The bet

Instacart wins this market by shipping one trusted agent at a time, each tied to a dollar a retailer can see in its own P&L.

Grocery runs on a 1.7% net margin, the lowest since 2019 (FMI 2024, via Grocery Dive). Most of the leak is operational: shelves go empty, fresh food spoils, and orders get mis-picked. I would start with fresh shrink, because that is where a competitor has already proven up to a 25% reduction and where Instacart's shopper data plus shelf vision can beat them.

The product is not the model. The product is trust. A retailer hands ordering decisions to an agent only when it can watch the agent, override it, and read the ROI.

The problem

A retailer loses margin in three places at once, and each one is large relative to that 1.7% net margin.

$129.5B
North American grocery out-of-stock per year (IHL Group, via RetailWire)
$112B
U.S. retail shrink, 1.6% of sales, with perishable departments a leading driver of grocery shrink (NRF 2023)
$196.4B
In-store inefficiencies cost U.S. retailers in 2026, 6.4% of gross sales, up from 5.5% in 2025 and 4.5% in 2024 (PR Newswire, 2025)

Out-of-stock

On-shelf availability routinely runs below target, and dips during the exact promotion windows when trade spend is highest (ECR / FMI, via Grocery Dive). The root cause is bad data, not lazy planners: up to 60% of inventory records are inaccurate, which creates "phantom inventory" where the system thinks a shelf is full and never reorders.

Shrink and waste

Roughly 30% of grocery food goes unsold each year, and perishable departments are a leading driver of grocery shrink (NRF 2023). At a 1.7% net margin, every 0.1% of sales recovered from shrink drops straight to the bottom line.

Fulfillment and picking

Instacart's own orders show that on average at least one item is replaced (Instacart Q4 2025 earnings call). That is a retention and margin risk Instacart can see in a way no traditional warehouse system can.

Why today's workarounds fail

Planners log into dashboards, eyeball spreadsheets, and walk aisles. Incumbent supply-chain software (Blue Yonder, RELEX) forecasts from the retailer's own order and POS history. Both inherit the 60% inaccurate-records problem, because they cannot see the real shelf. The status quo is the true competitor, and it has zero switching cost. Any agent has to be clearly better than a planner with a spreadsheet, not marginally better.

Who it is for

This is a B2B product. The person who uses it daily is not the person who signs the contract, and the agent has to speak to both.

JTBD is short for "job to be done," the underlying goal a buyer is hiring the product to accomplish.

daily user. Decides order quantities and markdowns per SKU		 "When I order fresh, help me not run out and not throw out, without me babysitting a dashboard." An agent that over-orders once on a big SKU and wastes a pallet. Trust is gone.

The agent has to satisfy the daily user (low effort, never embarrassing), the champion (visible margin recovery), the gatekeeper (safe and integrable), and the buyer (a defensible ROI number). A demo that wins only the data scientist loses the deal.

Why this problem

The team is small (2 PMs, 7 engineers) and cannot build out-of-stock, shrink, and fulfillment agents at once. So the first question is which problem to fund first.

RICE scores each option on Reach, Impact, Confidence, and Effort, then ranks by (Reach x Impact x Confidence) / Effort.

Candidate first bet Reach Impact Confidence Effort Read
Fresh shrink agent Every grocery banner has fresh departments High. Fresh is a leading driver of shrink; 0.1% of sales recovered hits net margin directly High. A competitor already proved up to 25% shrink cuts Medium. Needs POS, waste logs, shelf signal, bounded to fresh Build first
Out-of-stock / replenishment agent Whole store, very high reach High lost-sales recovery Medium. Phantom inventory and autonomous ordering raise the trust bar fastest High. Touches whole-store data and write-access to ordering Second

Why fresh shrink first. The value is least in doubt. Afresh reported up to a 25% shrink reduction, 3% sales uplift, 7% higher inventory turns, and 70% year-over-year revenue growth in 2025 across more than 12,500 departments (Afresh, via PR Newswire, April 2026), including a nationwide Albertsons fresh rollout completed in October 2025 (PR Newswire, Oct 2025). A proven market de-risks Confidence, the effort is bounded because fresh is one part of the store, and it is exactly where Instacart's data moat changes the answer: Afresh forecasts from a retailer's own history, while Instacart adds real-time shelf state from Store View computer vision and a live demand signal from 340M+ orders.

Approach

I would treat three directions as bets to validate, not a fixed plan, and decide between them with pilots.

  • H1, advisory first. The agent recommends order quantities and markdown timing; a department manager approves. Lowest trust barrier, fastest to ship, and it generates the override data that trains autonomy. This is also the pattern Instacart has already shipped through the Advantage Solutions partnership (PR Newswire, Sept 2025). I would start here.
  • H2, bounded autonomy. Once advisory accuracy is proven, let the agent auto-act below a configurable dollar threshold and escalate above it. This is where the labor savings and the bigger ROI sit, but it raises the trust bar, so it is earned, not assumed.
  • H3, the data-moat differentiator. Fuse Store View shelf state and the order-demand signal into the forecast. This is the bet that separates Instacart from Afresh and Blue Yonder. I would A/B the moat-enriched forecast against a history-only baseline inside the pilot to prove the lift is real and worth the integration cost.

The decision rule is the same across all three: a more autonomous or more data-rich version only graduates if it beats the simpler version on net shrink recovered, after subtracting the cost of its errors.

What I'd ship first

The MVP is the smallest test of the biggest assumption: that Instacart's shelf-and-demand data produces forecasts good enough that a fresh manager trusts and acts on them.

v0: an advisory fresh-ordering agent for two to three departments in one regional pilot retailer

  • One scope. Produce, plus one or two other fresh departments. Daily SKU-level order-quantity recommendations and markdown-timing flags.
  • Human-in-the-loop by default. Every recommendation is approved or overridden by the department manager. Every override is logged with a reason code.
  • An ROI panel from day one. Shrink dollars, waste pounds, and availability tracked against a pre-pilot baseline and a control set of stores.
  • A data-readiness gate before any model work. A minimum data contract (POS scan rates, waste and markdown logs, delivery schedules, Store View shelf state) at an agreed latency, with a graceful-degradation rule for when feeds are late or dirty.

What it deliberately leaves out: whole-store replenishment, autonomous ordering, multi-retailer rollout, the fulfillment agent, and international deployment. All real, all later. v0 exists to prove the dollar and earn the trust.

Metrics

The North Star is the metric that proves the product moved the retailer's P&L, because that is what renews a contract and unlocks the next one.

North Star: net margin dollars recovered per store per period, attributable to the agent and provable against a control group. A North Star alone can be gamed (you can cut shrink by under-ordering and just create out-of-stocks), so it sits in a tree of input metrics that show how the value is created and guardrails that catch the agent harming something else.

Eval framework for the AI itself

A recommendation is "right" when it reduces shrink without creating an out-of-stock. The two failure modes have different costs: a false positive (over-order) becomes waste; a false negative (under-order) becomes a missed sale and an empty shelf. I would set the agent's confidence threshold from those asymmetric costs, not from raw accuracy, and hold a golden set of historical days to test changes before they ship. The bar to act autonomously is not "accurate enough," it is "the expected dollar value of acting beats the expected dollar cost of being wrong."

Business impact

This work sits inside Instacart's enterprise pivot, where the growth thesis is recurring B2B revenue that is not tied to the per-order take rate. The numbers below are anchored to Instacart's reported figures.

  • It feeds the highest-growth, highest-margin line. Instacart reported Q1 2026 revenue of $1.019B, up 14% year over year, with advertising and other revenue growing 16% to $286M, its fastest growth in that segment since Q3 2023 (Instacart Q1 2026 results). Enterprise AI is positioned as the next leg of that line: recurring SaaS and platform fees retailers cannot build themselves.
  • The moat is already deployed. Store View shelf vision is live (Good Food Holdings), and Cart Assistant is launching with Kroger in its iOS app and Sprouts is collaborating on Cart Assistant (Instacart enterprise AI launch, Nov 2025). The Advantage Solutions human-in-the-loop shelf-visibility partnership plans to expand in 2026 (PR Newswire, Sept 2025). The fresh agent would sit on infrastructure that already exists, which shortens time to first revenue.
  • The expansion path is global. The Instaleap acquisition added nearly 100 grocery retailers across nearly 30 countries (Instacart acquires Instaleap, April 2026), which is the land-and-expand surface once the first agent proves its ROI domestically.

The revenue hypothesis I would own: pilot-to-contract conversion is the leading indicator, and a defensible per-store margin-recovery dollar is what converts a pilot into a multi-year, multi-department, multi-region contract.

Data readiness and a clean baseline come before any model tuning, because in enterprise the first quarter of a pilot is won or lost on data integration, not model accuracy.

Questions

The answers below would sharpen the first 90 days. These are what I would ask the team early.

  1. Which of the three problems (out-of-stock, shrink, or fulfillment) is the funded first bet, and is that already decided or open?
  2. What is the commercial model: per-store SaaS fee, per-retailer subscription, outcome-based pricing on margin recovered, or bundled into Storefront Pro? This sets the unit-economics model I would own.
  3. With Kroger and Sprouts as pioneer retailers, are there paying contracts and signed data-sharing agreements, or pilots? That changes how greenfield the first 90 days really are.
  4. How should this agent interact with Carrot Ads revenue, given that out-of-stock shelf data is also valuable to CPG advertisers? There may be a second revenue stream from the same signal.
  5. Post-Instaleap, is international deployment expected for the first product, or is the mandate to prove the model domestically first? The integration complexity is very different.
  6. What is the human-in-the-loop bar leadership is comfortable with for autonomous ordering, and what is the appetite for the trust and governance investment that requires?
