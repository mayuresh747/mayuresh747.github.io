The bet

Here's my bet: what decides whether an Outmarket workflow becomes a daily habit or gets abandoned is not raw speed, it is trust.

So I would make trust the product, not a feature bolted on the side. I'd instrument every workflow to show where the AI's answer came from, flag the outputs it is unsure about, and put a fast human-review step exactly where a wrong answer would cause real harm at binding. The goal is to move workflows from "tried once" to "daily ritual" and to lift the share of pilots that convert to production use.

The problem

My read: the industry's failure mode is not that AI is too slow, it is that people do not trust it enough to let it leave the pilot.

The pattern is stark. Over 90% of insurers are exploring or testing AI, but only 22% have fully deployed it in production (Insurance Thought Leadership, 2026). The same report is blunt about why: "Models that lacked transparency struggled to gain adoption. Explainability, not speed, proved essential." Only 38% of property-and-casualty insurers say they capture AI value at scale in their core workflows (Pibit, 2026).

Who hurts, and what it costs

  • Who hurts. The daily users at a brokerage: customer service representatives (CSRs), account managers, producers (the people who sell and service policies), and operations leads. They live inside their agency management system (AMS), the software of record for every policy.
  • What they struggle with. Comparing carrier quotes, checking a policy against what was quoted, generating a certificate of insurance (COI), and pulling data out of carrier PDFs. Slow, repetitive, and easy to get wrong. Manual submission intake alone runs 60 to 140 minutes per submission across triage, classification, extraction, validation, appetite check, and routing (Pibit, 2026).
  • Root cause. The work depends on unstructured documents that vary by carrier. An AI can read them fast, but if it reads one field wrong and the user cannot see that it did, the user stops trusting the whole tool.
  • Workarounds today. Double-checking every AI output by hand, or falling back to spreadsheets and manual comparison. Both erase the time savings the tool promised.
  • Why the workarounds fail, and the cost. A user who re-verifies everything is not faster, just more anxious. A user who blindly accepts a wrong output ships a bad policy and pays for it at binding. Either way the workflow gets run once and abandoned, and the account never expands. For a company whose growth depends on brokerages going live fast and expanding usage, an abandoned workflow is lost revenue that never shows up as a complaint.
Outmarket's own figures Outmarket reports its Compare Quotes workflow cuts quote comparison from roughly 2 hours to 4 minutes, and that customers see up to a 65% reduction in errors and omissions through AI-assisted comparison (product page), (homepage). Those are Outmarket's own figures, not independently audited. I would treat them as the target to protect, and I'd worry about the workflows that do not hit that bar yet.

Who it is for

I'd be building for two different people: the one who runs the workflow and the one who buys it. The product has to win both.

Here is the job I'd design for:

The job to be done When I am comparing three carrier quotes for a client renewal, I want to trust the summary in front of me without re-reading every PDF, so I can advise the client today instead of tomorrow and not get blamed for a coverage gap later.

That job has three layers. The functional layer is finishing the comparison fast. The emotional layer is not feeling anxious that the AI missed something. The social layer is looking sharp and reliable in front of the client and the agency owner. In insurance, the emotional and social layers drive adoption as much as the functional one, because the cost of one visible mistake is high.

Approach

I don't yet know which trust mechanism moves adoption most, so I'd frame three bets and let evidence pick.

  1. Source attribution first

    Every AI-derived field shows the exact place in the carrier document it came from, one click away. Bet: users abandon workflows because they cannot verify a number, and showing the source removes the need to re-read the whole PDF. This is closest to what Outmarket already claims on its homepage: "Every data point cited to its source" (homepage).

  2. Confidence and exception routing

    The AI marks low-confidence outputs and routes only those to a human, so users review the 10% that is risky instead of 100% out of anxiety. Bet: the trust problem is really an attention problem, and telling users where to look is worth more than showing every source.

  3. Human-review checkpoint at the harm point

    A required review step placed only where a wrong answer causes downstream damage (for example, before write-back to the AMS at binding). Bet: trust is won by proving the tool refuses to guess where guessing is expensive.

How I'd decide between them. Run all three as lightweight variants on one high-volume workflow, and watch two behaviors that reveal distrust directly. The re-run rate (a user running the same workflow again because they did not believe the first result) and the override rate (how often users correct a field). The variant that lowers re-runs and abandonment without slowing the workflow to a crawl wins. My prior is that attribution plus targeted confidence flags beats a blanket review step, because a review step on everything just recreates the manual work. But that is a bet to test, not a conclusion.

What I'd ship first

I'd start with the smallest test of the biggest assumption: a trust layer on the single most-run workflow, not a trust layer everywhere.

The biggest assumption is that visible source attribution plus confidence flags will move a real behavior (fewer re-runs, less abandonment) on a workflow users already run a lot. So the MVP is:

  • One workflow. The highest-volume one in the current base. Compare Quotes is the likely candidate given it is Outmarket's headline claim, but I'd confirm with usage data in week one rather than assume.
  • Source attribution on every AI-derived field. Click a field, see the exact spot in the carrier document it was pulled from.
  • Confidence flags on low-certainty fields. The output visibly marks what it is unsure about, so the user's eye goes there first.
  • One-click correction that is captured. When a user fixes a field, that correction is logged. This gives me the override signal and a stream of real errors to improve the workflow.

What it deliberately leaves out. No new workflows. No new lines of business (benefits, specialty). No custom-builder governance. No full human-review-queue product. Those are all more valuable after I know the trust layer changes behavior. Shipping them first would spread effort across surface area while the core question, "do users trust the output," stays unanswered.

Metrics

I'd pick one number that only rises when a user genuinely trusted the output, then guard it against the ways it could be gamed.

North Star: weekly trusted workflow completions per active user. A "trusted completion" is a workflow a user finished and acted on (moved forward, wrote back to the AMS) without re-running it out of doubt. I picked it because it rises only when a user got real value (they used the result), it moves before revenue (adoption precedes renewal and expansion), and it is a number the team can move directly through the trust layer.

This ladders to the business. Retention and expansion across the 250-plus brokerage base (Outmarket, 2026) depend on daily use sticking. Outmarket's headline claim of 12 to 15 hours saved per person per week (homepage) is Outmarket's own figure, and it only holds if users actually run and trust the workflows week after week, which is exactly what this metric tracks.

Guardrail and anti-metrics

Guardrail: AMS write-back error rate

Speed and adoption must not rise on the back of silent bad data landing in the system of record. If write-back errors climb, the trust layer has failed at the point that matters most.

Anti-metric: time-in-tool

More minutes in Outmarket is not a win. A user stuck re-reading outputs spends more time and trusts less. I would not celebrate engagement time.

Anti-metric: raw workflow run count

A workflow re-run three times out of doubt counts as three runs but signals a problem. I'd separate trusted completions from anxious repeats.

30 / 60 / 90

I'd spend the first month listening and learning the domain, the second sharpening the problem with real data and shipping a quick win, the third validating the trust bet.

Days 0 to 30: listen, map the domain, and read the data

Listen
  • Sit with CSRs, account managers, and operations leads at several brokerages and watch them run workflows live. I'd use the Mom Test discipline: ask what they did the last time they hit a problem, not whether they would like a feature.
  • Pull usage data to find which workflows are daily rituals and which get run once and dropped. Abandonment and re-run rates are my first read on where trust breaks.
  • Build my own insurance-domain map: how a quote-to-bind lifecycle works, what a COI is for, how a submission moves, where a wrong field causes real harm. I'd pair with customer success and go-to-market to compress this.
  • Learn the current AI evaluation setup: how the team decides an output is "good" today, and where source attribution and confidence already exist.

Days 30 to 60: a quick win, and sharpen the problem

Sharpen
  • Ship one small, visible improvement to the highest-abandonment workflow, most likely a source-attribution fix that removes a specific re-read users complained about. Early credibility with users and engineering.
  • Set the baseline: measure re-run, override, and abandonment rates on the target workflow so the MVP has something to beat.
  • Align stakeholders on the trust-first sequence: bring the usage evidence to engineering and go-to-market so the roadmap call is shared, not imposed.

Days 60 to 90: ship and validate the first bet

Validate
  • Ship the MVP trust layer on the highest-volume workflow as an A/B test against the current experience.
  • Read the result against the North Star and guardrails. Did trusted completions rise? Did re-runs and abandonment fall? Did write-back errors stay flat?
  • Propose the next quarter's roadmap from evidence: which trust mechanism to standardize across workflows, and whether expansion into a new line is ready to start.

Risks

The biggest risks I see are in the work, not the plan. Here is what I'd test in week one.

  • The trust bet might be wrong. Maybe users abandon workflows for a reason other than distrust, like the output landing in the wrong place or the workflow being slow. Validate first: before building anything, interview users who abandoned a workflow and read the re-run data to confirm distrust is the real driver.
  • Source attribution might not be enough. Showing a source link is only useful if users click it and it resolves their doubt. Validate first: instrument source-link usage in the MVP and check whether clicking it lowers the re-run rate.
  • Carrier document variance could break accuracy. Real carrier PDFs vary far more than test files, and the bar users expect is high: a competitor's case study reports roughly 99% accuracy on submissions within three months (FurtherAI, 2026). Validate first: measure the override rate on real customer documents, not a clean test set, and treat corrections as the signal to tune.
  • AMS write-back is a silent failure surface. Applied Epic, AMS360, HawkSoft, and Nexsure each have different schemas and permissions; a field-mapping error is invisible until binding. Validate first: define an integration test that checks a completed workflow round-trips correctly into each AMS before I trust any adoption number.
  • New-line expansion could thin quality. Outmarket is expanding into benefits and specialty, which have different document types and terminology. Validate first: hold the quality bar on existing lines as a gate, and require a defined quality benchmark for a new line before its pilot starts.
  • A confidence flag is only as good as the confidence estimate. If the AI is confidently wrong, the flag misleads. Validate first: check flagged-versus-actual error alignment on a labeled review set before shipping confidence flags widely.

Questions

These answers would change my plan, so I'd ask them early.

  1. Which workflows have the highest abandonment or re-run rates today, and is that already visible in the data or does it need instrumentation first?
  2. How does the team currently decide an AI output is "good" for a workflow step, and is there a labeled review set I can measure against?
  3. How mature is the benefits workflow tooling versus commercial P&C, and is a benefits pilot expected on the near-term roadmap?
  4. When a workflow writes back to an AMS, how are field-mapping errors caught today, and how often do they reach binding?
  5. Does Outmarket get structured data feeds from carriers, or is comparison built on parsing carrier PDFs? The technical risk profile differs a lot between the two.
  6. For the plain-English custom workflow builder, what governs the quality of user-generated workflows today, and is that a near-term priority or a later one?
