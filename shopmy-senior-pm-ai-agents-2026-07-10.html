The bet

My bet is that ShopMy's agent fleet already does real work, but there is no shared way to know when it does that work reliably.

I would build the evaluation and triage layer first, so every fix is decided from transcripts and reliability data instead of a hunch. That is what earns trust from the internal teams, brands, and creators the agents serve, and it protects ShopMy's "humans over algorithms" promise by drawing the autonomy line with evidence.

The problem

I read the failure modes in the job description as one root problem: the fleet grew faster than the measurement around it.

The job description names the symptoms plainly: "quality drift, confusing delegation, weak eval coverage, and agents being stretched beyond their shape" (ShopMy JD). The root cause underneath them is the same one: there is no shared definition of "good" per agent, no reliability tracking, and no systematic read of real transcripts. You cannot catch drift you do not measure.

Who hurts

Internal ops and support teams who are handed agent output, plus the brand clients and creators who receive that output. The role sits "between the people an agent serves and the agentic system itself" (ShopMy JD).

Why today's workarounds fail

  • Spot-checking outputs by hand does not scale across a fleet.
  • Reacting when someone complains means the failure has already reached a paying brand or creator.
  • A polished, best-case demo is not a reliability number. It hides the failure that hits on the second try.

Why a stumble is expensive

Failures here are externally visible to paying brands and creators, not just internal staff. That erodes the "curated by the obsessed, not the algorithm" trust that is the whole brand, and it wastes engineering time on the wrong fixes. It also lands in a live feature race: LTK shipped its own AI chatbot in February 2026 (NetInfluencer), and even Amazon's CEO says most AI shopping agents "lack personalization and often provide inaccurate pricing and delivery estimates" (Modern Retail). Trust is the unsolved problem industry-wide, so a stumble is expensive.

Who it is for

The three users here have different jobs, and I would keep them separate, because collapsing them is how an agent gets stretched beyond its shape.

Internal operator · primary user

Take the repetitive work, reliably

The ops or support person who uses agents to get real work done, such as vetting creators, onboarding brands, or matching campaigns. The specific tasks are not public, so I treat these as examples to confirm in week one.

When I am handed more work than I can do by hand, I want an agent to reliably take the repetitive parts, so I can spend my judgment on the parts that need a human.

Brand client · external user, economic buyer

Accurate, on-brand automation

Brands pay for the network and expect accurate, on-brand automation. Their trust is the revenue base: ShopMy tells brands they see "an average 5x return" (ShopMy for brands).

When I run a creator program on ShopMy, I want the platform's automated help to be accurate and on-brand, so I keep giving ShopMy more of my spend.

Creator · external user

Sound like me, and be right

Creators earn "10% to 30% on sales you drive" (ShopMy for creators), so a bad automated recommendation costs them money and credibility.

When ShopMy automates part of my work, I want it to sound like me and be right, so I keep earning and do not get embarrassed.

The internal buyer · who funds this

A new function that pays for itself

The product and engineering leadership funding this new function. There is also a brand-thesis owner: the company's president publicly champions human taste over algorithms, noting that many creators "come from stylist or editor backgrounds; they have made a living monetizing their taste" (Tubefilter). Whatever I ship cannot undercut that.

Approach

Trust is the goal, and I would test three ways to get there. These are not mutually exclusive, so the decision is about sequence.

  • H1 · measurement firstDefine "good" per agent in plain English, build graders, track reliability, and read transcripts. Decide every fix from data.
  • H2 · boundary firstDraw the line between where full agent autonomy is fine (internal, backend) and where a human stays in the loop (client and shopper facing). This matches ShopMy's own Noir bet, where AI synthesizes the data but "the resulting recommendations are made by humans" (Tubefilter).
  • H3 · orchestration firstFix how agents hand work to each other so the fleet stops dropping or duplicating tasks.

How I would decide: H1 is the prerequisite. You cannot set boundaries (H2) or fix handoffs (H3) rationally without a way to measure them. So I would run H1 first and let it feed the other two, validated on one live, high-traffic agent before generalizing.

The triage rule the JD asks for, own the agent fix, route the engineering gap, escalate the product ambiguity, runs on top of the measurement layer:

Ship first

One agent proves the loop before I touch the fleet.

The biggest assumption is that a lightweight eval and triage loop on a single agent will catch real failures that hand spot-checks miss, and change what actually gets fixed. The smallest test of that, on the one highest-volume or highest-stakes agent that touches paying brands or creators:

  1. Co-write a plain-language "what good looks like" spec with the team that owns the agent.
  2. Build a small eval set from real past runs, labeled by humans.
  3. Stand up three grader types: code-based checks for hard rules (format, boundary violations), a model-based judge for quality and on-brand tone, and human review for the ambiguous cases. Current best practice defines exactly these three grader types (Anthropic, 2026).
  4. Report reliability as "succeeds every time" (pass^k), not "succeeds at least once" (pass@k), and always read a sample of transcripts. The same guidance warns: "we do not take eval scores at face value until someone digs into the details of the eval and reads some transcripts" (Anthropic, 2026).
  5. Run the triage flow above on every failure the loop surfaces.

What it deliberately leaves out: the rest of the fleet, orchestration and handoff fixes, any new agent capability, and a polished dashboard. One agent, one loop, real transcripts.

Why this first: highest reach because the loop becomes a reusable template, it de-risks every later bet, and the effort is weeks rather than quarters.

Metrics

I would chase reliable, accepted work, not how much the fleet automates.

North Star: trusted agent task completion rate

The share of real tasks the fleet completes to the "good" bar consistently and that the receiving user accepts. It passes the three tests: value (if it rises, users and brands genuinely got trustworthy work), leading (it moves before complaints and churn), and actionable (the team can move it with prompt, boundary, and eval fixes).

Guardrails and anti-metrics

Do not optimize raw automation rate or task volume. Both look good while quality drops.
Never report pass@k best-case success alone. It overstates quality.
Do not drive the human-override rate to zero on client-facing work. That would remove the human check that protects the brand.
An eval score is not "green" until someone has read the transcripts behind it.

Business impact

The money case rests on numbers ShopMy already reports, and I would size the rest with the team's real take-rate and GMV data.

  • ShopMy drives "over $1B in annual platform sales" and promises brands "an average 5x return" (PR Newswire) (ShopMy for brands). That return depends on brands trusting the platform enough to keep and grow spend. If agents that touch brand programs are unreliable, that is a direct retention risk on the revenue base, and a reliability number is how I would watch it.
  • A trustworthy fleet is leverage. Agents that reliably do repetitive work let ShopMy serve "1,200+ premium brand partners" and "185,000+ tastemakers" (PR Newswire) without adding headcount in a straight line. Outside analysis flags that ShopMy's take rate on roughly $1B GMV is undisclosed (Affiverse), which makes agent leverage a margin lever, not just a cost line.
  • In the race with LTK, which reports "$6 billion in annual retail sales powered" (NetInfluencer), the differentiator is not "we have AI." It is "our automation is trustworthy and stays human where it counts." Reliability is what makes that claim real.

30 / 60 / 90

My first ninety days move from listening, to a working loop, to a proposed roadmap.

0 to 30 · listen and map

Learn the fleet and pick one agent

Interview the internal teams and, where possible, the brand and creator clients each agent serves. Inventory the live fleet: what each agent does, who it serves, and where failures show up. Read real transcripts. Sit with engineering to learn the orchestration and tooling and where PM-owned prompt and eval work hands off to ML-owned model and infra work. Pick the one agent for the MVP. Score the four RICE candidates with real numbers.

30 to 60 · quick win and sharper problem

Ship the loop on one agent

Ship the eval and triage loop on that one agent. Produce the first reliability baseline and a shared "what good looks like" spec. Show a real failure the loop caught that spot-checks missed. Draft a first autonomy boundary map, where humans stay in the loop, with the brand thesis in mind. Align stakeholders on the own, route, escalate buckets.

60 to 90 · generalize and propose

Template the loop and propose the roadmap

Roll the loop to two or three more agents using the template. Set a fleet-level reliability baseline and drift alerts. Propose the roadmap: which agents to harden, where orchestration and handoffs need work, and where to route engineering, with a refreshed RICE ranking behind it.

Risks

I test the shaky parts of this plan early rather than defend them later.

RiskHow I would test it early
The biggest failure mode may be orchestration and handoffs, not single-agent quality I would read cross-agent transcripts in week one before locking the MVP onto one agent.
"Good" is subjective and the team may not agree I would co-write the spec and label a small eval set together. The disagreement rate on labels tells me whether the bar is real.
A model-based judge can be wrong or gameable I would check its agreement against human labels on a sample before trusting its scores, per the "read the transcripts" rule.
More automation can collide with the "human curation" brand I would validate the autonomy boundary with brand and product leadership early and treat full client-facing autonomy as escalate-by-default.
My ROI ranking is directional until I have real per-agent cost and business numbers I would get those and re-score RICE.
Measurement can turn into busywork I would require every eval to map to a fix or a routing decision, and kill the ones that do not.

Questions

I would let a few answers reshape my sequencing.

  1. How many agents are live today, what does each one do, and who exactly does each serve (internal, brand, creator)?
  2. What orchestration framework and tooling run the fleet, and where does PM-owned prompt and eval work hand off to the ML-engineer-owned model and infra work?
  3. Which agent failures have already reached a paying brand or creator, and how did you find out?
  4. Is there a current definition of "good" for any agent, and who owns it today?
  5. Where does leadership want the hard line between full agent autonomy and human-in-the-loop, given the Noir positioning?
  6. What is the real per-agent cost and business impact, so I can prioritize on ROI rather than guesswork?
