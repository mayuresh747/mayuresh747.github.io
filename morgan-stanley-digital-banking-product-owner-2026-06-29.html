The bet

Keep idle client cash inside Morgan Stanley and E*TRADE, one tap at a time.

Uninvested cash that earns almost nothing is the easiest reason for a client to open an account somewhere else. My bet: I would own and ship an embedded AI cash assistant inside the E*TRADE and Morgan Stanley apps that spots a client's idle cash, shows them their real options in plain numbers, and moves it into one of the firm's own higher-yield products in one tap. The point is not to chase the highest rate on the market. It is to keep the deposit in-house instead of losing it to Fidelity or a neobank, which protects the deposit base, the interest income built on it, and the wider client relationship.

This is a squad-level product area, not a division strategy. I would own the requirements, the delivery, and the metrics for this one experience, working alongside the senior product managers who set the broader roadmap.

The problem

The firm already sells competitive savings products. A client with cash at a low rate is just never shown, at the right moment, that a better in-house option is one tap away.

Bank Deposit Program and sweep

The Bank Deposit Program (BDP) automatically moves a client's uninvested brokerage cash into deposits at affiliated banks. That automatic move is called a "sweep." The headline yield on swept cash is low.

What it costs the client, and the firm

A consolidated class action alleges the firm paid 0.01% to 0.5% on swept cash while earning roughly $8 billion in 2023 by deploying those balances (Kurta Law), and the SEC closed its own investigation in March 2025 without enforcement (The Wealth Advisor). Those rate figures are plaintiff allegations, not a confirmed current rate. The legal outcome matters less than this: the gap is visible to any client who comparison-shops. Fidelity's Cash Management Account sweeps cash into a government money market fund earning roughly 4.5% to 5.0% as of 2026, with up to $5 million in FDIC coverage across 20 partner banks (WealthVieu). Other brokerage and fintech competitors advertise comparable or higher yields on idle cash.

The root cause

The firm already sells good in-house alternatives. E*TRADE Premium Savings runs a 4.00% promotional rate for six months on a 3.50% base, with enhanced FDIC coverage up to $500K individual and $1M joint (Premium Savings), and E*TRADE CDs pay around 4.10% (NerdWallet). The 3.50% base is from Bankrate's review and changes often (Bankrate). The problem is not the absence of a good product. It is that the client is never shown, at the right moment and in plain terms, that the better in-house option exists and takes one tap.

The workaround today, and why it fails

A motivated client reads a rate page, compares, and manually opens a savings account or buys a CD. Most never do. The friction is too high and the prompt never comes. So the cash either sits idle at a low rate or it leaves for a competitor. Either way the firm loses: idle cash at a low rate erodes trust, and cash that leaves erodes the $419 billion deposit base (Q1 2026 earnings call).

Who it is for

I would start with the self-directed E*TRADE client, because the population is large and the whole journey can be completed digitally without an advisor in the loop.

Job to be done

A "job to be done" is the underlying goal a person is really trying to accomplish, not the feature they happen to use.

The self-directed segment holds $1.56 trillion in assets, up 21% year over year (Financial Planning, Q1 2026). Their job:

When I notice cash building up in my account, I want to make it earn a fair return without a lot of research or risk, so I can feel I am not leaving money on the table.

That job has three layers. The functional job: move idle cash to a better rate. The emotional job: stop feeling slightly ripped off. The social job: feel like a smart steward of my own money. The emotional layer is what sends a client to open a Fidelity account, and it is what an in-app nudge can address before they leave.

Others in the frame

  • The advisor-served Morgan Stanley client, reached later through the advisor's view rather than direct nudges, because that relationship has a human in the loop and different suitability rules.
  • The internal decision-makers I would build with, not just for: compliance and legal (what we may show and say), fraud and risk (any movement of money), and treasury or finance (the interest-income tradeoff). In a regulated bank each of these partners can stop a feature, so they are part of the design from day one.

Why this problem

I scored the things this team could fund with RICE. The cash assistant wins on wide reach, high impact, and only moderate effort.

RICE in one line

Score is Reach times Impact times Confidence, divided by Effort, so expensive low-confidence work falls down the list. Reach and Effort are measured; Impact (0.25 to 3) and Confidence (50%, 80%, 100%) are judgment calls.

These are my prioritization estimates, not Morgan Stanley figures.

The cash assistant leans on products that already exist (Premium Savings, CDs, Preferred Savings) rather than new infrastructure. Platform unification scores high on impact but is a multi-year program other work depends on, so I would build the assistant in a portable way and let unification absorb it over time, not wait for it. Raising the headline sweep rate is a treasury and pricing decision, not a product build, and it narrows the firm's own margin, so I leave it off the backlog and treat retention as the lever I can actually pull.

Approach

I would not commit to one design before testing. I would run three directions as bets and start with the one that tests the core behavior at the lowest risk.

01 Proactive nudge

Detect idle cash above a threshold and surface a single card: "You have $X earning roughly Y%. Move it to Premium Savings at 4.00% for six months." One comparison, one action. Fastest to build, highest behavioral risk (will clients act).

02 On-demand cash check

A "review my cash" screen the client opens themselves, showing all their cash and the in-house options ranked by rate. Lower compliance risk because the client asked, but it reaches fewer people because they have to seek it out.

03 Conversational AI

A natural-language assistant that answers "what should I do with my cash?" Highest emotional payoff and the strongest match to the embedded AI the role calls for, but the hardest to make compliant and the easiest to get wrong.

How I would decide: start with the version that tests the core behavior at the lowest risk, then layer on the harder directions only once the behavior and the compliance path are proven. That points to a proactive nudge built on a comparison screen the client can also open on demand, with conversational AI held back until the suggestion logic is trusted.

What I'd ship first

The smallest honest test of the biggest assumption: will a client move idle cash into an in-house product when shown a clear, compliant in-app prompt?

  • One platform: E*TRADE self-directed, where the move is fully digital and the population is large.
  • One trigger: settled cash above a set threshold sitting in the low-yield sweep for more than N days.
  • One action: a comparison card (current rate versus Premium Savings) with one-tap move, using disclosure language pre-cleared by compliance and legal.
  • One cohort: a contained client group, instrumented end to end.

What it deliberately leaves out

The conversational AI, the Morgan Stanley advisor-side view, multi-product optimization across CDs and Preferred Savings, and cross-platform parity. Those come only after the core move-rate is proven. By RICE logic this is first because it is the lowest-effort slice that still tests the highest-impact assumption, and it puts a real number on client appetite before anyone funds the larger build.

Metrics

North Star: dollars of idle cash activated into a higher-yield in-house product per quarter. The client genuinely wins, it moves before revenue does, and the team can influence it directly.

The guardrails matter so we do not win the headline number by causing harm. More prompts is not the goal: if opt-out or complaint rates climb, we are creating fatigue, not value. Cash moved out to a competitor is never success, so net deposit outflow is the failure this feature exists to prevent. Every AI-generated suggestion must clear a suitability and accuracy check before it ships, because a prompt that misstates a balance or rate destroys trust and creates regulatory exposure. And we track net interest income so we are moving cash that was at risk of leaving, not cash that was content where it sat.

Business impact

Deposits are the engine. Keeping at-risk cash in-house at a thinner margin beats losing it to Fidelity outright, and against a $419 billion base even a small retained share is material.

Period-end deposits stood at $419 billion and bank lending balances at $186 billion, with household lending penetration at 18%, up from 14% five years earlier (Q1 2026 earnings call). Every deposit that stays in-house keeps earning the firm net interest income and keeps the client in the relationship that drives the 18% household figure the business tracks for growth. The logic is straightforward: a client who banks where they invests is harder to poach and refers more assets.

That reframes the cash assistant in business terms. Moving a client from a near-zero sweep into a 3.50% to 4.00% in-house savings account narrows the firm's spread on that balance. But losing the balance to a competitor entirely loses the spread, the deposit, and the household. My hypothesis is that retaining at-risk cash at a thinner margin beats losing it outright, and the MVP exists to put a real number on that tradeoff before the firm commits to it at scale.

30 / 60 / 90

Days 0 to 30

Listen and map the constraints

  • Meet the squads, the UX Design and Research team, and the compliance, legal, fraud, risk, and treasury partners.
  • Read the BDP, Premium Savings, and CD product docs and the current disclosure language.
  • Pull the data on idle-cash balances, current activation rates, and outflow to competitors.
  • Tear down the live E*TRADE and Morgan Stanley cash experiences, and learn from what the Generative AI team has already shipped, e.g. AI Debrief reaching about 98% advisor adoption (adoption case) and the firm opening its workplace wealth platform to external AI agents in June 2026 (American Bazaar).
Days 30 to 60

A quick win and a sharpened plan

  • Ship the low-risk quick win: an improved "your cash options" comparison screen, no AI, mostly disclosure work.
  • Write the MVP user stories and acceptance criteria with engineering and compliance.
  • Run usability tests on the nudge concept and set the deposit and activation baselines.
  • Align the senior PMs and partner teams on scope.
Days 60 to 90

Ship and validate the first bet

  • Release the MVP nudge to the contained cohort and instrument the funnel.
  • Read the move rate and net deposits retained.
  • Present the results to the product managers and leadership.
  • Propose the roadmap: CDs and Preferred Savings, then the advisor view, then conversational AI once the logic is trusted.

Risks

These are open questions about the proposed work, in priority order, and the first thing I would test for each.

Open assumption in the work What I would validate first
Compliance and suitability is make-or-break Week one, confirm exactly what we may show and say, what disclosure language is required, and the approval path. If compliance forbids any comparison, the whole approach changes.
Net interest income tradeoff With treasury and finance, identify which idle-cash segments are genuinely at flight risk versus content where they are, so we move the at-risk dollars and not all of them.
AI accuracy and groundedness Set accuracy and groundedness thresholds and require a human-reviewable suggestion log before any AI-generated text reaches a client.
Client behavior Clients may ignore or distrust nudges. Test through usability sessions and the MVP cohort move-rate, not assumptions.
Platform fragmentation Building E*TRADE-first risks rework on the Morgan Stanley side. Measure how far the two cash data models differ and design the trigger logic to be portable from the start.
Data and event availability Real-time idle-cash detection needs the right feeds and event hooks. Confirm with engineering early that the trigger can fire when it should.

Questions

What I would ask the team in the first conversations.

  1. What does compliance currently allow in a client-facing rate comparison and in AI-generated financial suggestions, and what is the approval path?
  2. How much idle cash sits in the low-yield sweep across E*TRADE self-directed versus advisor-managed accounts, and how much leaves to outside competitors each quarter?
  3. What has the Generative AI team already built for client-facing experiences that I can reuse rather than rebuild?
  4. What is treasury's view on the tradeoff: is keeping a deposit in-house at a thinner margin preferred to losing it entirely?
  5. Where is the Morgan Stanley and E*TRADE unification roadmap today, and should I build cross-platform from day one or E*TRADE-first?
  6. What are the current digital banking adoption and deposit-retention baselines I would be measured against?
