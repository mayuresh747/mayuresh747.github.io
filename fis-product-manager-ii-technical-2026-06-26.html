The bet

Get FIS's state clients off skimmable magnetic-stripe EBT cards and onto chip plus layered fraud controls, fast and repeatably.

California, with FIS as the processor, cut reported benefit theft 83% (California Governor, Jan 2026). FIS is the only processor USDA FNS has approved to broadly deploy chip EBT cards (WYPR, Oct 2025). I would turn that proof and that approval into a repeatable onboarding playbook that wins new state contracts and renews the ones FIS already holds.

EBTElectronic Benefit Transfer. The card that carries food and cash benefits like SNAP, TANF, and WIC.
Magnetic stripeThe old brown band on the back of a card. Easy to copy.
ChipThe small metal square that is far harder to clone.
SkimmingWhen a thief hides a reader on a checkout terminal to steal card data.

The problem

Benefit theft is at crisis scale, victims now have no recourse, and the proven fix is a product job, not a policy job.

Who hurts

Two groups. SNAP and cash-benefit recipients lose money they need for food. State agencies, which are FIS's actual clients, take the political heat from legislators and constituents.

What it costs

Propel estimates about $349 million in SNAP benefits stolen in the first half of 2025, and roughly $600 million across all of 2025 (Nextgov, Dec 2025). 43% of theft in 2025 happened out of state, up from 35% in 2024 (Propel).

Why it keeps happening

EBT cards still run on 1970s-era magnetic-stripe rails. Thieves install skimmers on checkout terminals, clone the cards, and drain the balance (GAO).

Why the old workaround fails

Until recently, states could replace stolen benefits with federal funds. That authority expired December 20, 2024. Benefits stolen on or after December 21, 2024 are no longer eligible for federal replacement (GAO-25-107964, May 2025). The safety net is gone, so the only remaining lever is preventing theft through the product.

The cost to the business

State EBT contracts are multi-year and awarded by competitive bid. Losing one forecloses revenue for 5 to 10 years, and the states most exposed to theft headlines are the ones most ready to switch processors. FIS won Maryland (up to $38.4M, about 943,000 recipients) and Massachusetts in 2024 and 2025 through exactly this kind of competition (Maryland Matters, Oct 2025) (Mass.gov, Apr 2025).

Who it is for

This is a product sold to government, where the daily user and the buyer are different people, so the demo has to speak to both.

I would design for three roles. The job-to-be-done is the job each role is hiring the product to do, stated as a situation, a motivation, and an outcome.

RoleWhoThe job they hire the product to do
Cardholder (daily user)A SNAP, TANF, or WIC recipientWhen I get my benefits, I want them safe and locked unless I am spending, so I can feed my family without losing the money to a thief.
State program owner (the buyer)A state DHS or DSS director and program staffWhen theft makes headlines and federal replacement is gone, I want a fix I can show legislators and roll out fast, so I can protect recipients and keep my agency out of crisis.

The emotional and social jobs drive the buying decision here. The state owner is not buying chip cards. They are buying the ability to stand in front of a legislature and say the problem is handled. That shapes every demo and every status report.

Approach

I would treat chip migration as a repeatable product, not a one-off project per state, and decide between directions with a pilot.

Three directions I would explore, and how I would choose.

  1. Bespoke per-state migration. Treat each state as a custom build. Familiar, but slow and expensive, and it does not scale across the deployment wave coming in 2026 to 2028.
  2. Standard playbook with state-specific configuration. One repeatable onboarding sequence (card issuance, cardholder communication, retailer terminal testing, parallel run, go-live) with a thin configuration layer for each state's rules. My lead hypothesis. It reuses the California and Massachusetts learnings and shortens every later onboarding.
  3. Controls-first, chip-second. Ship the layered fraud controls default-on everywhere immediately, then migrate chip behind them. Lowers theft fastest while the longer chip rollout proceeds.

Directions 2 and 3 are not exclusive. I would run controls-first as the immediate move and stand up the playbook in parallel, then prove the playbook on the next live onboarding before generalizing it. The deciding evidence is the next state cutover: did the playbook cut onboarding time and incidents against the Massachusetts baseline.

Here is the layered model I would make the default, with the chip migration sitting behind it as the structural fix.

What I would ship first

Turn the existing fraud controls on by default for one state. It is cheap, fast, and proves the impact before the longer chip rollout.

The biggest assumption is that default-on layered controls cut theft sharply without breaking legitimate spending. California's forced PIN resets alone cut theft about 80% among affected accounts (California Governor, Jan 2026), and adoption of card-locking jumped 500% once states promoted it in-app (Propel). South Carolina already auto-placed cards in a high-risk-block status (SC DSS). The mechanism is proven; the open question is the default-on configuration and its false-decline rate.

The MVP

With one willing state client, switch out-of-state blocking and forced PIN reset to opt-out (on by default), surface card lock prominently in the ebtEDGE app, and measure theft and false declines for 60 days against that state's own baseline.

Why first, in RICE terms

Highest reach (every cardholder in the state), high impact (proven), high confidence, and low effort because the controls already exist in ebtEDGE (FIS ebtEDGE).

What it deliberately leaves out

The full chip card reissue, retailer terminal upgrades, and real-time blocking. Those are the next layers. The MVP buys protection now and produces the evidence that de-risks the bigger spend.

Metrics

The North Star is the share of FIS-served cardholders protected by chip plus layered controls, with guardrails that stop us from blocking the people we are trying to help.

North Star: percent of FIS-served EBT cardholders covered by chip cards and default-on layered controls. It passes the three tests. Value: if it rises, theft falls and recipients keep their benefits. Leading: it moves before contract renewals and before the next theft headline. Actionable: the team directly drives it through onboarding and feature defaults.

Supporting measures

Theft reduction on the served base, state client satisfaction, onboarding on-time and incident-free rate, enhancement delivery against commitment, competitive win and retain rate, and cost-estimate and margin accuracy on bids.

Guardrails and anti-metrics

Do not let false declines on legitimate transactions rise. A control that blocks a hungry family at the register fails the mission even if the theft number looks good.

Do not let onboarding incidents rise as we speed up.

Do not chase raw card-lock toggles as a vanity number. What matters is theft prevented per protected account.

30/60/90

Listen and find the real constraints first, ship the cheap proven win next, then prove the playbook and propose the roadmap.

Questions

The answers to these would change my sequencing.

  1. Which states are closest to a re-bid or renewal decision in the next 18 months, and which are most exposed to theft headlines today?
  2. What is the current false-decline rate on out-of-state blocking, and have any states pushed back on it?
  3. Where are the bottlenecks in the Massachusetts and California onboardings that I should design out of the playbook?
  4. What does the FNS chip-approval process require of FIS per state, and what is the real lead time?
  5. How is cost estimation done today for bids, and where have past estimates missed on margin?
  6. For real-time transaction blocking, what data and partners would we need, and is there an existing pilot to learn from?
