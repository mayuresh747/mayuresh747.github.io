The bet

Make Scale the default place frontier labs go to train finance agents.

I would ship the first high-fidelity finance RL environment with verifiers that grade the auditable reasoning chain, not just the final number. Finance is unclaimed territory in the environments race, the buying demand is already funded, and the moat is exactly what makes the work hard: realistic data plus expert verification. That combination is a defensible vertical, not a one-off contract.

What an RL environment is

A reinforcement learning environment is a software simulation of a real workflow where an AI agent practices a task, gets a verifiable reward signal for doing it correctly, and improves through repeated attempts. Scale describes these as simulated worlds where agents train on realistic tool-use workflows without the risk of production systems (Scale, RL environments). My job in this role is to build those simulations for finance.

The problem

Three forces line up here, and they all point at the same opening.

Finance agents fail at rates no bank would accept

On the FinGAIA benchmark of 407 expert-validated tasks across securities, banking, insurance, and asset management, the best agent scored 48.9% overall accuracy against an 84.7% human expert baseline, a gap of 35.8 points (FinGAIA, arXiv). Portfolio fund allocation accuracy was 29.2% and fraud detection 22.8%. On BigFinanceBench of 928 expert-authored research questions, the best systems stay below 60% rubric score and below 45% final-answer accuracy (BigFinanceBench, arXiv). The failure modes are specific: hallucinated comparable transactions, terminology used wrongly, and reasoning that reaches a number with no auditable derivation.

The training infrastructure to close that gap barely exists

The capability race has moved to RL on verifiable tasks, and one lab discussed spending over $1 billion on RL environments within a single year (TechCrunch, 2025). Nearly half of Scale's new data-training projects in 2025 already involved RL environments, and training models in more realistic reinforcement learning environments is named as a 2026 priority (Scale, RL environments) (Scale, 2026 strategy). Yet almost no production-grade finance environments exist, because finance success is non-binary. A model can be structurally right and assumption-wrong at the same time.

The competitive moment makes finance strategically urgent

After the $14.3B Meta investment for a 49% non-voting stake at a $29B valuation, Scale's largest customer pulled back and several labs began exploring alternatives (Sacra). Competitors are moving fast: one rival reached a $10B valuation with a $500M ARR run rate (TechCrunch, 2025), another surpassed Scale in 2024 revenue (The AI Insider, 2025). No competitor has announced a dedicated finance environments product. Finance is where Scale can win clean, new lab relationships that the Meta overhang does not touch.

The cost of not acting: the labs build finance environments in-house or a rival claims the vertical, and Scale loses the highest-value, hardest-to-replicate corner of the market it helped create.

Who it is for

A job to be done is the outcome a customer is really trying to achieve when they buy. Two customers matter here, and they are not the same.

Primary buyer: the post-training research team at a frontier lab

They sign six-to-seven-figure quarterly contracts for environments, and exclusive deals cost roughly 4 to 5 times more than non-exclusive ones (Epoch AI FAQ). Their job: help me raise my model's finance-agent capability on benchmarks my own customers care about, without having to build and verify finance simulations in-house. They do not want a vendor. They want a co-design partner who helps define how the training infrastructure should evolve (Data Gravity).

Primary user inside the buyer: the research engineer who runs RL training loops

Their job: give me an environment with a clean difficulty gradient and a reward signal I can trust, so my model learns instead of reward-hacking. They reward smooth difficulty, a minimum 2 to 3% pass rate so there is a learning signal, long-horizon multi-step tasks, and verifiers that resist gaming (Epoch AI FAQ).

Downstream demand signal: enterprise finance teams deploying agents now

One bank co-developed agents with a frontier lab for trade accounting, reconciliation, and client onboarding, reporting 30% faster onboarding (MLQ.ai, 2026). Another runs 400-plus production AI use cases (AI Business Weekly, 2026). These deployments are the reason labs need better finance training data in the first place. They are not my direct buyer, but they are the proof that lab demand is real and durable.

I would not start with comps extraction even though it is cheap and high-confidence, because models are already decent at extraction so the capability headroom is small. The investment memo build wins on impact and reach; the financial model build is the close second and carries the hardest, most defensible verifier problem. Concentrating on one deep workflow beats spreading thin across five shallow ones, because a competitor can copy breadth fast but cannot copy a verifier that encodes real banking judgment.

Approach

I would treat the first quarter as a test of three bets, not a fixed plan.

Hypothesis 1: the verifier is the product, not the simulation

The hard part is not cloning the spreadsheet UI, it is the reward signal. Scale's own framing is that verifiers must check real, measurable changes in the system, not just whether an agent produced plausible text (Scale, RL environments). For finance I would build layered verifiers that grade the derivation chain: source-tracing (did the agent pull the figure from the right document), calculation correctness, assumption defensibility (is the terminal growth rate justifiable for the industry), and hallucination checks for the FinGAIA failure modes. I would decide this is right if labs pay more for the same environment when the verifier depth increases.

Hypothesis 2: synthetic plus seeded real data beats either alone

Real memos and data rooms are protected by NDA and securities law, so I cannot just buy them. I would source a small spine of real, legally clearable artifacts to anchor realism, then generate synthetic data rooms that reproduce the ambiguity of real work: incomplete records, conflicting figures, judgment calls. I would decide between sourcing strategies by measuring which produces the harder, less reward-hackable tasks.

Hypothesis 3: co-design with one lab beats building in a vacuum

Labs want thought partners. I would anchor the first environment to one design-partner lab's actual capability gap rather than guess, so the difficulty gradient and task list match what they are training against. I would validate this by whether a partner commits engineering time to co-define the task spec.

How I would decide between directions: I would run small task batches against a current best model and keep the workflow where the pass rate sits in the learnable 2 to 3% to roughly 40% band, the gap is widest, and the verifier survives an internal reward-hacking attempt.

What I'd ship first

The smallest test of the biggest assumption: one workflow, one design-partner lab, verifier-first.

The MVP: an investment-memo-build environment seeded from a single realistic, legally clearable data room. The agent works from raw documents toward a memo with a supporting model. Success is graded by a layered verifier across roughly 8 to 12 subtasks (source-tracing, comp selection, calculation, assumption defensibility, narrative consistency), not by a single final score. The biggest assumption it tests: that I can build a finance verifier labs trust enough to train on and renew.

Light RICE reasoning for why this first: highest impact (long-horizon, largest documented gap), high reach (memos and data rooms are explicitly in the JD), and it forces the hardest problem, verification, early, while effort stays bounded by limiting scope to one workflow and one data room.

What it deliberately leaves out

Breadth across IB, PE, public markets, and FP&A; a full library of data rooms; trade accounting and reconciliation; and enterprise self-serve. Those come only after one verifier proves it produces measurable capability lift. Shipping breadth before a trusted verifier would be the expensive mistake.

Metrics

North Star: verified capability lift per environment, the improvement a design-partner model shows on the matching finance benchmark after training in the environment.

This ties directly to Scale's own claim that models trained in its RL environments show measurable benchmark gains (Scale, RL environments), and to what makes a lab renew.

The guardrails matter as much as the North Star. If reward-hacking rate climbs, the capability lift is fake. If verifier cost per task runs away (tasks already cost $200 to $2,000 each, with complex clones near $300,000, per Epoch AI), the vertical stops being economic. If hallucinated facts pass the verifier, the environment trains the wrong behavior. I would refuse to count a benchmark gain that comes with a rising reward-hacking rate.

30 / 60 / 90

Days 0 to 30

Listen and map the gap

  • Meet the lab research teams Scale already works with; learn which finance capabilities they train against and where agents break.
  • Audit whatever finance environment assets Scale has today.
  • Sit with internal finance experts and SEAL's evaluation practices, 450-plus evaluations across 50-plus models in 2025 (Scale).
  • Map FinGAIA and BigFinanceBench failure modes to buildable workflows. Pick the design-partner lab.
Days 30 to 60

A sharp first cut and alignment

  • Spec the investment-memo MVP with the design partner.
  • Stand up the layered verifier; run small task batches against a current best model to confirm the difficulty gradient and gap.
  • Pressure-test the verifier with an internal reward-hacking attempt.
  • Lock the data-sourcing approach (legally clearable spine plus synthetic generation). Align engineering, research, and any enterprise-sales overlap on scope.
Days 60 to 90

Ship and baseline

  • Deliver the first environment to the design partner; set the capability-lift baseline and instrument the guardrails.
  • Bring back evidence: pass rates, verifier-versus-expert agreement, early benchmark movement.
  • Propose the 6-month finance roadmap (next two workflows, exclusivity strategy, data-sourcing partnership plan) grounded in what the first environment showed.
