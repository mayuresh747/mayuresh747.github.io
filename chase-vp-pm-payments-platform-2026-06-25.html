Product Strategy Pitch

Mayuresh More

How I would solve the consumer payments problem for the VP Product Manager, Payments Platform role at JPMorgan Chase.

Prepared June 2026 · Wallets & Lending Innovations, Consumer & Community Banking

The bet

Win the checkout surface Chase already controls, then extend that credential outward.

Chase owns the rails, the scale, and the trust. But it captures only 5.2% of its customers' $450 billion in addressable commerce spend, and its bank wallet Paze has 165 million enrolled cards with almost no consumer habit.

My bet: turn bank-grade trust and the rails Chase owns into a payment customers reach for without thinking. Win the surface Chase already controls first, then extend that credential into the agentic commerce layer, all built inside the risk and controls environment.

The problem

Chase has the infrastructure. It does not yet have the everyday habit, and the checkout surface is being claimed by Big Tech, PayPal, and now AI agents.

Chase moved $7 trillion in consumer payments volume in 2025, growing at an 11% CAGR since 2019 (Marianne Lake 2025 letter). It has 75 million digital users and the #1 ranked US digital banking platform. The rails are not the problem. Five gaps are.

  • The commerce capture gap. Chase captures 5.2% of the $450 billion its own cardholders spend, up from 3.0% in 2021. The stated target is 10% (Lake 2025 letter). That gap is roughly $22 billion in annual spend Chase touches but does not yet own at checkout.
  • The distribution versus habit gap. Paze, the bank consortium e-commerce wallet Chase co-owns, has 165 million eligible cards and processor integrations with Fiserv, Worldpay, Nuvei, and ACI. But consumer awareness sits far below PayPal's 82% awareness and 37% online preference (Spreedly 2026). The rails exist. The reflex to use them does not.
  • Someone else owns the checkout surface. Apple Pay carries 54% of mobile taps at the point of sale, and PayPal is the default online checkout brand (Spreedly 2026). Chase cards are tokenized into those wallets, but the device maker and the wallet brand own the customer relationship and the network token economics.
  • The credential layer is shifting to AI agents. On June 10, 2026, Visa and Mastercard both launched frameworks that let AI agents complete purchases using scoped network credentials, and JPMorgan's own research projects agents will handle 15% to 25% of US e-commerce by 2030 (JPMorgan, Visa, Digital Commerce 360). If network agent tokens become the default credential, the bank wallet risks becoming invisible.
  • Speed versus controls. Every new payment product at Chase requires sign-off from Risk, Controls, Legal, and Compliance before any customer touches it, under live regulator scrutiny. A new wallet is not just an engineering build. It is a controls translation problem. This is what makes the role hard and why the JD names controls as a co-equal responsibility.

The cost of standing still: a customer who reaches for Apple Pay or PayPal at checkout is a customer whose commerce spend, interchange, and data Chase does not capture, and a relationship the network or device maker deepens instead of Chase.

Who it is for

Three different people decide whether a Chase payment experience wins. I would design for all three, not just the cardholder.

Jobs-to-be-Done framing: a job is the progress a person is trying to make in a moment. People hire a payment method to make that progress and fire the old one when something does it better.

PersonaThe job they hire a payment forWhat "winning" looks like
The everyday cardholder
(user)		 "When I am at checkout, I want to pay in one tap without re-entering my card or worrying about fraud, so I can finish and move on." They reach for the Chase or Paze button by reflex, not the device wallet.
The merchant
(acceptance side)		 "When I build my checkout, I want a payment option that lifts approval rates and cuts fraud without a wallet fee, so I sell more and lose less." Paze tokenization already shows roughly 3% authorization lift, 28% fraud reduction, and up to 10 bps interchange savings (Spreedly 2026). I would make that the merchant pitch.
The risk and controls gatekeeper
(internal decision-maker)		 "When a new payment product ships, I want it to clear Risk, Legal, and Compliance with traceable controls, so we launch without regulatory exposure." The product spec is controls-ready, so sign-off is fast, not a fight.

The functional job is "pay quickly." The emotional job, and the one Chase can win on, is "pay safely, from a name I trust." That is the bank's structural advantage over a fintech, and it is the wedge I would build on.

Why this problem: prioritization

I would not chase every payment idea at once. I would fund the bets where Chase controls distribution first, then the credential bets that protect the future.

Why this ranking

  • Bet 1 ranks first because Chase controls its own digital surface, so distribution is not a fight. Closing even part of the 5.2% to 10% gap on $450 billion is the single largest prize, and the JD is written for exactly this 0-to-1 surface.
  • Bet 2 ranks second because the rails and 165 million cards already exist. The work is habit, not infrastructure, which is cheaper than a ground-up build.
  • Fraud and controls is treated as a spine, not a feature. It does not compete on RICE. Everything else depends on it clearing Risk and Compliance, so it stays in scope regardless of score.
  • Agentic readiness ranks high on impact but low on confidence, because Marianne Lake has said publicly she does not expect consumer agentic commerce to take off soon (Banking Dive 2026). I would fund it as a small readiness bet, not a big build, so Chase stays in the credential layer without overspending on a market that is not here yet.

Approach

Win an owned surface first, prove the habit, then extend the credential outward. Decide between directions with evidence, not opinion.

I would explore three directions and sequence them deliberately:

  • Owned-surface checkout first. Build the quick-checkout habit where Chase controls distribution, inside the Chase app and Chase-issued card experiences. This is the lowest-distribution-risk way to test whether a bank-branded surface can earn reflexive use.
  • Extend to Paze surfacing. Use what I learn about habit on the owned surface to drive Paze where Chase controls the prompt, then push outward to third-party checkouts. The same trust story, a wider footprint.
  • Agentic credential readiness, scoped small. Make the Chase credential agent-ready so that when consumer agentic commerce arrives, Chase is in the credential layer, not bypassed by network tokens.

How I would decide between them: I would treat each as a bet with a kill criterion. If the owned-surface checkout does not lift repeat usage in a contained test, I would not pour effort into Paze surfacing on the same assumption. Habit-formation evidence on the smallest surface decides whether the bigger bets are funded.

What I would ship first

The smallest test of the biggest assumption is whether a Chase-branded button earns reflexive reuse on a surface Chase already controls.

The riskiest assumption is not "can we build it." Paze proves Chase can build payment rails. The riskiest assumption is "will customers form the habit of reaching for a bank payment button when Apple Pay and PayPal are right there."

So I would ship a Chase quick-checkout experience to a contained cohort of Chase digital users, on a surface Chase controls end to end, with one-tap pay using already-tokenized cards.

  • What it tests: repeat usage rate, not just first use. First use is curiosity. Reuse is habit.
  • What it deliberately leaves out: broad third-party merchant rollout, agentic credential support, and net-new money-movement rails. Those wait until the habit signal is real.
  • Why this first (RICE logic): highest reach on a surface with the lowest distribution risk, and it produces the one piece of evidence every later bet depends on. It is the cheapest way to learn the most.

How I would measure success

One North Star tied to Chase's stated business goal, with fraud and controls as guardrails that can stop a launch, not just metrics to admire.

North Star: commerce spend capture rate on Chase cards, the move from 5.2% toward the 10% target (Lake 2025 letter). It passes the three tests: if it rises the customer genuinely chose Chase at checkout, it moves before revenue, and the team can influence it.

  • Leading indicators (move first): repeat usage rate, checkout click-through, authorization rate. Target authorization above 85%, since each point is revenue-accretive at Chase scale (Primer 2026).
  • Guardrails (treated as veto, not vanity): fraud rate below 15 bps on card-not-present, chargeback rate below 0.5%, 99.99% uptime, and controls sign-off on time. A win on adoption that breaks a guardrail is not a win. Fraud and controls can halt a launch.
  • Anti-metric I would not chase: raw transaction count or first-time enrollments alone. A spike in one-time use with no reuse is a marketing result, not a habit. Repeat usage is the number that matters.

Business impact

Closing the commerce-capture gap is the largest revenue lever this role touches, and it also reduces a real single-point dependency.

Three places this work moves the numbers:

  • The capture gap is the prize. Moving from 5.2% to 10% of $450 billion in addressable commerce spend is roughly $22 billion in annual spend Chase would own at checkout instead of ceding to Apple Pay or PayPal (Lake 2025 letter). Even a fraction of that gap is material at Chase's $7 trillion volume base.
  • Authorization lift compounds. Paze tokenization shows roughly 3% authorization lift; at Chase scale each point of approval rate is directly revenue-accretive (Spreedly 2026).
  • It reduces interchange concentration risk. The Credit Card Competition Act is alive in 2026 with executive backing and would force routing competition on issuers with over $100 billion in assets, putting interchange and rewards economics in play (KTS Law 2026). Building commerce-capture and embedded-lending revenue surfaces reduces Chase's reliance on a single interchange-driven engine.

30 / 60 / 90 day plan

Listen and find the real constraints, then sharpen the problem with evidence, then ship the first contained test inside controls.

Days 0 to 30: listen and map the constraints

  • Meet Product, Technology, Design, and Analytics partners, and sit down early with Risk, Controls, Legal, and Compliance to learn the controls path before I design anything.
  • Confirm exact team scope: which products Wallets & Lending Innovations owns (Paze surfacing, Chase quick checkout, Pay Over Time, Pay in 4), since the JD is written broadly.
  • Pull the real funnel data: where Chase customers drop at checkout, what the current capture rate and authorization rates are, and where Paze sits today.
  • Meet the junior PMs I would mentor and learn what they own.

Days 30 to 60: a quick win and a sharpened problem

  • Ship one quick win that is cheap and controls-friendly, likely Pay Over Time or Paze surfacing on a Chase-controlled prompt, to build delivery trust and learn the sign-off rhythm.
  • Replace my RICE judgment calls with real numbers and re-rank the bets with Analytics.
  • Align stakeholders on the North Star (commerce capture rate) and the guardrails, and get Risk and Compliance to agree the controls criteria up front, not at the gate.

Days 60 to 90: ship and validate the first bet

  • Ship the contained quick-checkout MVP to a defined cohort with controls sign-off, a baseline measured, and clear success and kill criteria.
  • Set the repeat-usage baseline and read the habit signal.
  • Bring a sequenced roadmap to Sunil and Josh: what the habit evidence says about funding Paze surfacing and the agentic readiness bet next.
