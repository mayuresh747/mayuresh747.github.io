The bet

Make the Chase card the default, lowest-friction way to pay at checkout.

Chase captures only 5.2% of the $450 billion its own cardholders spend with merchants, against a stated 10% target (Marianne Lake 2025 letter).

My bet: close that gap by making the Chase card the easiest way to pay at the moment a shopper picks how to pay, starting with Paze. The same work hedges Chase against the two threats now reshaping payments: a possible law that would loosen the fees Chase earns on card purchases, and AI shopping assistants that could move the trusted payment identity from the bank to the card network.

The problem

Chase has the volume. The problem is what happens at the checkout, where the shopper chooses how to pay.

In 2025 Chase moved $7 trillion in consumer payments and serves 75 million digital users (Lake 2025 letter). The problem is not reach. It is the moment of payment.

  • Who hurts. Two parties. The Chase cardholder, who at checkout faces a familiar wall of options (Apple Pay, PayPal, type-the-card-number) and reaches for habit, not for Chase. And Chase itself, which loses the economics and the data when a shopper pays through a wallet that someone else controls.
  • What it costs. Chase wins 5.2% of $450 billion in addressable spend and wants 10%. That gap is roughly $22 billion in annual spend that runs on other ways to pay (Lake 2025 letter). Separately, 61% of shoppers abandoned a purchase in 2024 because their preferred way to pay was not offered (Spreedly 2026). Each abandoned cart is lost volume.
  • Root cause. At online checkout, the shopper's default is whatever is fastest and most familiar. PayPal has 82% consumer awareness and is the habit for online buying (Spreedly 2026). Paze, the bank-built online wallet Chase co-owns, has 165 million pre-enrolled cards but almost no consumer awareness. The cards are loaded. The habit is not.
  • The workaround that fails. The industry answer so far is to pre-enroll cards and wait. Paze proves that does not create usage on its own: the plumbing is in place at large payment processors, yet shoppers do not reach for it. Distribution is solved. Habit is not.

So the 0-to-1 task this role names is not building the payment plumbing. It is building the product that turns a loaded card into a reflex at the moment of payment.

Who it is for

"Job to be done" means the real job a person hires a product for, stated as a situation, a motivation, and an outcome. Two users matter here, and in payments the merchant is effectively a second customer who must say yes before the first one ever sees the option.

PersonaThe job they are hiring a payment forWhat makes them switch
The everyday Chase shopper primary When I am checking out online, I want to pay in one tap without re-typing my card, so I can finish fast and trust the charge is safe. One tap, no card number, visibly tied to Chase, and the fraud protection they already trust from their bank.
The online merchant second customer When a shopper reaches my checkout, I want them to complete the purchase and not bounce, so I keep the sale and pay less to accept it. A higher share of carts that convert, fewer fraud losses, and a lower cost to accept payment. Paze cites roughly a 3% lift in approved payments and up to 10 basis points of fee savings on online card sales (Spreedly 2026).

The deciding party is the merchant: if the merchant does not show the option, the shopper never gets the one-tap choice. So the product has to win both, and the merchant case is the harder, slower sell.

Of everything a VP on this team could fund, the checkout default is the only bet where Chase both controls the levers and moves the stated 10% goal directly.

Approach

Treat the path as a set of bets to test, not a fixed plan. Run the first two in parallel and let the data pick the winner.

  1. Win the bank-built online wallet (Paze) at the moment of payment. The cards are already enrolled and the large payment processors are integrated (Spreedly 2026). The open question is habit: will a Chase shopper choose it when shown? I would test this where Chase has the most control of the experience and the most trust to lend: Chase's own app and Chase-driven merchant placements.
  2. Make Chase's own app the easiest place to start a payment. When a shopper is already in the Chase app, the bank controls the full experience and carries the trust. I would test whether a one-tap pay-and-track flow there lifts repeat use more than a third-party placement does.
  3. Lean on embedded lending (Pay Over Time, Pay in 4) as the revenue hedge. This earns fee revenue that does not depend on card swipe fees, which matters if the Credit Card Competition Act passes (KTS Law 2026).

How I would decide: run the first two as small, instrumented tests in parallel and let the data on repeat usage and completed checkouts pick the winner. Whichever creates habit fastest gets the build. Lending runs alongside as a separate, steady line.

What I would ship first

The smallest test of the biggest assumption: that a Chase shopper, shown a one-tap Chase option at the right moment, will choose it and come back.

The MVP. A one-tap Chase pay-and-confirm flow at a small set of online checkouts where Chase has the most influence, plus the same flow inside the Chase app. A shopper pays in one tap with no typed card number, sees the charge tied to Chase, and gets the bank's fraud protection. I would instrument it heavily: who is shown the option, who taps it, who comes back.

Why this first, in RICE terms: it scores highest on Reach and Impact because it sits on the exact moment that decides the 10% goal, and Chase controls enough of the experience to learn fast. It deliberately leaves out:

  • A standalone marketing push to build broad awareness. That is expensive and slow, and I want proof that habit forms before spending on reach.
  • Building for AI shopping assistants. Important later, not the first test (see the 30/60/90 plan).
  • New money-movement plumbing. Zelle already carries consumer instant payments at scale and Chase co-owns it (Zelle 2025).

Metrics

North Star: weekly repeat Chase-initiated checkouts per active user. A one-time tap is not a win. Coming back is.

A North Star is the one number that best stands in for real value delivered. Repeat use is the proof of habit. It rises only when shoppers genuinely prefer paying with Chase, it moves before revenue does, and the team can influence it.

Targets from the payments literature: approval rate above 85% (each percentage point is revenue at Chase's scale), fraud below 15 basis points online, chargebacks below 0.5%, and uptime at 99.99%+ (Primer.io 2026; ClearlyPayments 2026).

Guardrails and the anti-metric. Fraud, chargebacks, uptime, and on-time controls sign-off cannot get worse while we chase adoption. The anti-metric I would name out loud: do not optimize for raw taps or time-in-app. A shopper who taps once and never returns, or who is nudged into spending they regret, is a loss dressed as a win.

Business impact

These are hypotheses tied to Chase's own figures, not promises.

  • Closing the commerce-capture gap is the headline. Moving from 5.2% toward the 10% target on $450 billion in addressable spend is the explicit goal this work serves (Lake 2025 letter). The roughly $22 billion in annual spend sitting in that gap is the prize.
  • Better approval rates are directly accretive. At Chase's payment scale, each percentage point of improvement in the share of payments approved is revenue (Primer.io 2026).
  • Embedded lending diversifies revenue away from card swipe fees. Pay Over Time already serves 6 million customers with $10.8 billion in originations (Lake 2025 letter). If the Credit Card Competition Act passes and loosens card-fee economics, fee-based lending revenue is a hedge (KTS Law 2026).

30 / 60 / 90

Listen and find constraints first, ship one quick win, then launch and validate the first bet.

In the first 30 days I would treat the controls process as a path to learn early, not a gate to fight at the end. The PM's job here is to translate innovation intent into a specification that Risk, Controls, Legal, and Compliance can approve. Learning that path in week one is what keeps the later launch on schedule.

Risks

Open assumptions about the work, and how I would test each in the first weeks.

Risk in the workWhy it mattersHow I would validate first
Habit may not form even when the option is shown. The whole thesis rests on shoppers coming back. Paze proves enrollment alone does not create usage (Spreedly 2026). Run a small instrumented pilot and watch repeat use, not first taps. Kill the placement if week-two return rate stays flat.
Merchant availability could be the real bottleneck. If merchants do not show the option, no shopper sees it. Confirm where Chase can actually place the option today, and measure the shown-the-option rate before measuring anything downstream.
Controls sign-off could stall momentum. Every new payment product needs Risk, Legal, and Compliance approval before launch, and regulator scrutiny on bank wallets is live (American Banker 2025). Bring controls partners in during design, not at the end. Track on-time sign-off as a first-class metric.
AI-assistant commerce timing is uncertain. If AI assistants' payment identities become the default for online buying, the bank's own wallet matters less. But Chase's CCB CEO has said consumer agent-led commerce is not taking off soon (Banking Dive 2026). Place a small, cheap option to keep Chase's payment identity usable by assistants. Do not over-invest until the funnel data justifies it.
Card-fee revenue could loosen. The Credit Card Competition Act has bipartisan and executive backing, though it has not passed (KTS Law 2026). Treat the embedded-lending revenue line as the hedge and keep it on the roadmap regardless of the checkout work.

Questions

What I would ask the team in week one to sharpen the first bet.

  1. Which products does Wallets & Lending Innovations actually own end to end: Paze placement, the Chase app checkout, Pay Over Time, the Zelle experience, or a subset? Scope changes the first bet.
  2. Where can Chase place a one-tap option at checkout today, on its own properties and in third-party checkouts, without new merchant agreements?
  3. What does the current controls sign-off path look like from idea to launch, and where does it usually slow down?
  4. What is the current baseline for shown-the-option rate, first-tap rate, and approval rate on Chase-initiated online checkouts?
  5. How is the team thinking about AI shopping assistants on the consumer side, given Visa and Mastercard both launched agent payment frameworks on June 10, 2026? (Digital Commerce 360 2026)
