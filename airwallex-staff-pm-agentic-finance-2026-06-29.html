The bet

T:0 wins or loses on one thing: will a founder let an AI agent touch their books, their taxes, and their money without checking its work.

My bet is that the road to general availability is not more automation first, it is trust first. I would build the human-in-the-loop and auditability layer as the core product, then expand what the agent does on its own as accuracy earns each new step. The fastest distribution path is the 676,000 businesses already on Airwallex, not net-new acquisition.

The problem

T:0 is meant to run a startup's whole finance function from day zero. The hard part is not the work. It is getting a founder to trust an agent that can be wrong.

T:0 launched in private beta alongside the Series H on June 25, 2026: bookkeeping, forecasting, taxes, compliance, and reporting, all AI-native.

Who hurts, and what it costs them

  • Founders running globally distributed startups juggle multi-currency books, cross-border payroll, and tax filings in more than one country at once.
  • The tools they use today, QuickBooks, Xero, and Gusto, were built for single-entity, single-currency companies. So the multi-entity work falls back on spreadsheets and a part-time accountant.
  • Spreadsheets do not reconcile across currencies. An accountant is slow and expensive. Neither closes the books in real time or tells a founder their cash position today across three entities.

The window is narrow

Every month T:0 sits in private beta is a month a rival can take the segment. Ramp shipped Applied AI in June 2026. Brex runs OpenAI's global spend. Puzzle auto-categorizes 98% of transactions for startup bookkeeping.

The root problem. An AI agent that touches money cannot guess. Large language models are nondeterministic: the same prompt can give different answers on different runs. FINRA's 2026 oversight report names AI agents acting without human validation, and weak auditability, as distinct risks. A wrong tax filing has a real owner and a real liability. So the question is not "how much can the agent do." It is "how does a founder know the agent is right, and what happens when it is not."

Who it is for

There are two people in the room, and they are not the same person.

Primary user: the founder, or first finance hire

At an early, globally distributed startup. This is the person doing the work today, in spreadsheets, late at night. What they are trying to get done:

Functional job

When I run a startup with entities in two or three countries, I want my books closed, my taxes handled, and my cash position clear without doing it by hand, so I can spend my time on the product.

Emotional job

I want to stop lying awake wondering whether a filing is late or a number is wrong.

Social job

I want to look buttoned-up and credible to my investors and my board when they ask about runway and burn.

Economic buyer: the founder still, or a head of finance by Series A

In a five-person company the user and the buyer are the same. By 30 people they split, and the buyer cares about control and audit trail, not just speed. The emotional and social jobs, looking credible and sleeping at night, are what actually drive a team to switch.

The status quo is the real competitor. A spreadsheet plus an accountant has zero switching cost and full trust. Beating that needs a step change, not a small improvement.

Of everything this team could fund first, I would put the trust and human-in-the-loop layer ahead of any new automated workflow.

Approach

I would not commit to one shape on day one. I would frame three directions as bets and run cheap tests to choose.

Direction A: agent-first, human-on-the-exception

The agent does the work and escalates only what it is unsure about. Ramp's agents auto-handle routine expenses and escalate only the 10 to 15% that need human judgment, and Ramp's policy agent claims 99% accuracy. This is the end state, but it assumes accuracy is already high enough to trust the default.

Direction B: human-first, agent-as-drafter

The agent prepares everything, a person approves before anything is committed. Slower, but the trust bar is low and a founder can watch the agent learn. This is where I would start a brand-new product, because it builds the confidence Direction A needs.

Direction C: per-workflow trust tiers

The agent acts on its own for low-risk, reversible work (categorizing a transaction) and asks for approval on high-risk, hard-to-reverse work (filing a return, moving money). The boundary moves per workflow as accuracy data comes in.

How I would decide: start at B for everything, then move each workflow to C and eventually A as its measured accuracy crosses a bar I would set with engineering and legal. The decision is driven by per-workflow accuracy data, not a one-time architecture call. This is the core PM judgment in the role: where the line between agent and human sits, and when it moves.

What I'd ship first

The MVP is the smallest test of the biggest assumption: that a founder will adopt an agent they supervise, then let it run more on its own as it earns trust.

Ship: automated bookkeeping for one narrow profile (say, a US-incorporated startup with a UK entity, already on Airwallex for payments), running in Tier 1: agent drafts, founder approves. Three things have to be in it.

  1. A review queue where the agent shows what it did and what it is unsure about.
  2. A plain rationale on every action, so the founder sees why the agent categorized a transaction the way it did. Ramp ships this as "every action comes with a rationale", and it is what makes the work auditable.
  3. A clean escalation and recovery path: how the agent says "I am not sure," and how the founder corrects it so it learns.

Why bookkeeping first, by RICE: high reach (every user needs it), high impact (it is the daily pain), and largely reversible (a miscategorized transaction can be fixed, unlike a filed return). That keeps trust risk low while we prove the loop. Tax and payroll come later, once bookkeeping accuracy clears the bar, because their errors are harder to undo.

What the MVP deliberately leaves out: autonomous money movement, tax filing, payroll, multi-language support, and the broad "startups globally" audience. One narrow profile, one workflow, one trust tier. We earn the right to add the next layer with accuracy data, not a roadmap promise.

Metrics

North Star: weekly active founders whose books are closed by the agent with no manual rework.

It is the single number that best proxies real value. It rises only when a founder genuinely got their finance work done without doing it by hand. It passes the three tests: if it rises the customer truly won, it moves before revenue, and the team can influence it.

Guardrails and anti-metrics matter as much here, because in autonomous finance a vanity number can hide a trust failure.

The trick with the guardrails: escalation rate should fall as the agent improves, but it should never hit zero, because zero escalation in autonomous finance means the agent stopped flagging things it should flag. Correction rate is the honest accuracy signal from real users, not a lab benchmark.

30 / 60 / 90

Listen first, sharpen the bet, then ship the supervised MVP and set a baseline.

0 to 30 days · Listen

Discovery and constraints

Run structured discovery with 15 to 20 founders and finance leads, half from the existing base, half net-new. I would use the Mom Test discipline: ask what they did the last time they closed their books, not whether they would use a tool. Read the private beta's usage and escalation data. Sit with the ML engineers on current per-workflow accuracy. Map the roadmap and find the real constraints, technical, legal, and organizational.

30 to 60 days · Sharpen and align

Define the bar, land a quick win

Pick the first narrow ICP from the data and write it down precisely. With engineering and legal, define what "accuracy" means per workflow and what a false positive versus a false negative costs. Set the bar that moves a workflow from Tier 1 to Tier 2. Quick win: instrument the beta so escalation rate and correction rate are visible, which they likely are not yet. Align engineering, design, data science, and commercial on the trust-first sequence.

60 to 90 days · Ship and baseline

Supervised MVP to design partners

Ship the supervised bookkeeping MVP to a small set of design-partner startups. Set the baseline on the North Star and the guardrails. Run the build-eval loop weekly with the ML team. Bring back a proposed roadmap grounded in real accuracy data: which workflow moves to which trust tier, and when.

Risks

These are the open assumptions in the proposed work. I would test the first three in week one.

Founders will not tolerate a supervised agent

The whole MVP rests on a founder approving the agent's work at first. If supervision feels like more work than a spreadsheet, the loop fails. Validate: in discovery, watch how design-partner founders react to a clickable supervised flow, and whether they come back unprompted.

Accuracy is not yet high enough to move past Tier 1

If bookkeeping accuracy stays low, no workflow ever earns autonomy and the product stalls at "drafts you check." Validate: measure per-workflow accuracy against a labeled review set in the first 30 days, before promising any roadmap.

Regulatory liability on tax and compliance

Tax filing is a regulated activity in many jurisdictions. If T:0 files a wrong return, the liability owner has to be defined before launch. Validate: get a legal and product framework on tax liability and human sign-off before any tax workflow leaves Tier 1.

The existing base does not convert

The wedge assumes existing customers will try a finance OS from their payments provider. They may see it as out of scope. Validate: run a small activation test into one segment and measure try rate and activation, not just clicks.

ICP too broad

"Startups globally" is too wide to reach product-market fit. A day-zero team in SF is a different user from a 50-person Series A in Singapore. Validate: commit to one narrow profile for the MVP and resist widening it until the loop works for that one.

Questions

Five questions for the team that would reshape the plan.

  1. What does the private-beta data already show on per-workflow accuracy and escalation rate? That single answer reshapes the whole sequence.
  2. Are we building on foundation-model APIs or fine-tuned proprietary models for tax and accounting, and who owns that call? It has multi-year cost and speed implications.
  3. Where does T:0's responsibility end and Airi's begin, given both were announced together? I want the scope boundary clear before I plan the roadmap.
  4. On the existing base, is the goal attach-and-retain or net-new acquisition first, and what does commercial expect on timeline?
  5. What is our stance when T:0's analysis suggests a customer should move payroll or restructure an entity in a way that reduces Airwallex revenue? I want the principle set before the agent surfaces it.
