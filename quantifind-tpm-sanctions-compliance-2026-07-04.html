Key terms, in plain words

OFAC
The US Treasury office that publishes sanctions lists and enforces them.
False positive
A screening alert that looked like a sanctioned party but is not one.
Entity resolution
Deciding whether two records point to the same real person or company.
UBO
Ultimate beneficial owner: the person who actually owns or controls a company.
Model-risk management
A bank's internal process for validating any model or AI before it can influence a decision.

The bet

My bet is that Quantifind wins the sanctions line fastest by fixing the one workflow that bleeds the most money first: false-positive alert review.

That is where analyst hours disappear, where Graphyte's matching already has an edge, and where a bank will pay to cut the load. The catch that makes it sellable: every alert the product clears has to arrive with an audit record a bank's model-risk team will sign off on. So the first product is not just faster triage. It is triage a regulator's examiner would accept.

The problem

The way I see it, a sanctions team's day is mostly spent clearing alerts that were never real.

  • Who hurts. Sanctions analysts and their compliance-operations leaders at large banks. Six of the world's top 10 Tier 1 banks already run Graphyte (Quantifind, June 2026), so the buyer is already inside the tent.
  • What. They drown in screening alerts that are overwhelmingly false matches.
  • Root cause. Legacy screening tools match names against static lists with fuzzy string logic. Common names, transliterations, and partial matches trip the filter constantly.
  • Workarounds, and why they fail. Teams throw analyst hours at it and tune match thresholds up and down. Raise the threshold and you risk missing a real hit, which is unacceptable. Lower it and you bury the team. Manual review does not scale as alert volumes and list counts keep climbing.

Who it is for

I count three people who touch a sanctions alert, and each is hiring the product for a different job; any one of them can stall a deal.

Job to be done · model-risk reviewer, the gatekeeper

"When a new AI tool wants to touch sanctions decisions, give me a reproducible, explainable record of every match and no-match call, so I can sign off that it meets our standards and survives an OFAC exam."

The three people I would design for
Persona The job they hire the product to do What they fear
Sanctions analyst
daily user		 Confirm fast whether an alert is a real match or noise, so I clear my queue without being the one who missed a true hit. Personal liability for a missed hit.
Compliance-operations lead
economic buyer		 Cut the noise without raising audit risk, so I hit my SLAs and can defend the program to regulators and the board. An enforcement action on my watch.
Model-risk / audit reviewer
gatekeeper		 Get a reproducible, explainable record of every call, so I can sign off that it meets our standards. Approving a black box she cannot defend.

The gatekeeper is the one most pitches forget. In a bank, she holds the veto. If the model-risk reviewer cannot explain and reproduce a decision, the analyst never gets to use the tool, no matter how good the matching is. That is why the audit record is a hard requirement, not a feature request.

A note on the 50% Rule, since it drives the UBO row. Any company that is 50 percent or more owned, directly or through intermediaries, by sanctioned parties is itself blocked, even with no name-list hit (ComplyAdvantage). Tracing that is the hardest and most defensible problem in this space. It is the right second move, not the first. I would not open a 0 to 1 line on the riskiest, longest build.

Approach

My plan is to keep two versions of the same first workflow alive until a design partner's model-risk team tells me which one they can actually accept.

  1. Direction A, auto-triage. The product recommends clear, escalate, or needs-judgment on each alert, and can eventually auto-clear obvious false positives. Highest payback. Highest trust bar.
  2. Direction B, analyst copilot. The product never auto-clears. It ranks the alert, pre-fills the dossier, and explains its reasoning, and the human still makes every call. Lower payback, but a far lower trust bar and faster adoption.
  3. Direction C, UBO tracing. Go straight for the moat. I would hold this as the next horizon, gated on A or B proving the trust model first.

How I would decide between A and B: run both in the same shadow-mode pilot. They share the matching engine and the audit record, so testing both costs little extra. The design partner's model-risk reviewer sets how far the automation is allowed to go. I let the customer's audit bar, not my preference, pick the aggressiveness.

What I'd ship first

The smallest thing I'd ship first is whatever answers the scariest question: will a bank trust AI to touch a sanctions decision at all?

The MVP: an explainable sanctions alert-triage layer, run in recommend-only shadow mode. On each screening alert, Graphyte's entity resolution and Name Science score the hit, recommend clear, escalate, or needs-judgment, and generate a disposition record that states why, on what data, and at what confidence, in the customer's own language. It runs side by side with the analyst's live process at one or two design-partner banks. It recommends; it does not yet decide.

Effort is contained because it reuses the matching engine and sits next to the existing Case Accelerator (Sept 2025) rather than duplicating it.

What it deliberately leaves out: auto-execution in production (recommend-only until recall and the audit record clear the model-risk bar), UBO auto-tracing (the second bet), real-time payment-path screening (a later latency problem), full case management (Case Accelerator already owns that; triage sits upstream), and list curation (OpenSanctions already handles the source data).

Metrics

I'd judge this on cleared noise that keeps its audit trail, never on speed alone.

The North Star is the auditable false-positive clearance rate: the share of false-positive alerts the product resolves with a disposition record the customer's model-risk team accepts, per analyst-hour. It rises only when the customer genuinely wins, less manual review with the trail intact. It moves before revenue, and the team can influence it directly.

The sacred guardrail: missed-hit rate. The false-negative rate against a labeled review set must stay at or below the customer's own manual baseline. In sanctions you never trade recall for throughput. A single missed true hit can undo every efficiency gain and then some. Two anti-metrics I would name so the team never chases them: raw alert-handling speed on its own (fast is worthless if it misses hits), and sheer volume of auto-clears (looks productive, can hide recall loss).

30 / 60 / 90

My first ninety days are mostly listening, then one contained pilot.

Days 0 to 30 · Listen and map
  • Ride along with sanctions analysts at two or three design-partner banks and watch real alert queues.
  • Sit with a model-risk reviewer to learn exactly what a sign-off requires, and map where the current screening tool breaks.
  • Read what I am building on: the entity-resolution engine, Case Accelerator, the OpenSanctions data layer.
  • Pull the baseline: false-positive rate, time per alert, current audit process.
Days 30 to 60 · Sharpen and commit
  • Write the MVP PRD with measurable goals and non-goals.
  • Lock the disposition-record schema with a real model-risk reviewer as a hard acceptance criterion.
  • Get one or two design partners committed, each with explicit success and kill criteria.
  • Align engineering and data science on the matching engine and the audit record.
Days 60 to 90 · Ship the shadow pilot
  • Run recommend-only alongside the analysts' live process.
  • Measure false-positive clearance, missed-hit rate against a labeled set, and audit-acceptance. Set the metric baseline.
  • Take the audit artifact to the customer's model-risk team for a mock validation.
  • Propose the roadmap, with UBO tracing as the sequenced second bet.

Risks

The things that could sink this are about trust and data, and I would test them in week one.

Banks may not accept AI clearing sanctions false positives, even in shadow mode.

Validate: get the model-risk sign-off bar written down in week one, and run recommend-only until it clears.

Matching output may not be explainable enough to survive an exam.

Validate: co-design the disposition record with a real model-risk reviewer against a real exam scenario.

Auto-clear quality depends on list, PEP, and entity-resolution recall. Garbage in means missed hits.

Validate: measure recall on a labeled review set before touching throughput. Recall is the gate.

The triage layer could overlap Case Accelerator.

Validate: map the boundary with the existing product team in week one, and position triage upstream of case management.

The wrong first design partner produces the wrong MVP.

Validate: pick a partner whose alert volume and audit maturity are representative, and set kill criteria up front.

Scope could drift toward UBO and real-time too early.

Validate: hold the line. The MVP is disposition only; everything else is roadmap.

Questions

  1. Is the sanctions line sold standalone, or bundled into existing KYC and AML accounts? This changes packaging, pricing, and the first target list.
  2. How does this sit next to Case Accelerator and the OpenSanctions data layer, and who owns the boundary?
  3. Which design-partner banks can we get in the room in the first 60 days, and what is their appetite for AI in a model-risk-governed workflow?
  4. How explainable is the matching engine today, and what does a disposition record currently look like to an examiner?
  5. What is the current sanctions false-positive baseline across deployed customers, and do we have labeled data to measure recall?
