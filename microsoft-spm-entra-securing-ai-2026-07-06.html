A few terms, in plain language

SWG (Secure Web Gateway)
The inline proxy every managed user and device routes through.
SSE (Security Service Edge)
The cloud layer that gateway sits in.
Shadow AI
Employees or agents using AI tools the company cannot see.
DLP (data loss prevention)
Stopping sensitive data from leaving.
MCP (Model Context Protocol)
The standard agents use to call tools and services.

01The bet

My bet: Microsoft wins securing AI by turning detection into safe adoption, not by out-detecting Zscaler on shadow AI.

I would convert Microsoft's identity and Microsoft 365 distribution into the default control point for AI traffic, so security teams move from blocking AI to governing it. Concretely, I would grow AI-control attach on the Entra Internet Access base by closing the gap between "we can see the AI risk" and "we can safely allow the AI."

02The problem

The people I would build for are drowning in AI they cannot see, and blunt blocking makes it worse.

  • Who hurts. Enterprise security teams, the CISO and the admins who run policy, whose employees and increasingly autonomous agents send corporate data to AI services outside any control.
  • What they struggle with. They cannot see which AI tools are in use, cannot stop sensitive data flowing into them, and have no way to govern agent-to-service traffic that does not come from a browser.
  • Root cause. AI adoption outran the controls. Personal accounts, browser sessions, and now agents bypass network controls built for human web traffic.
  • How they cope today. They block AI domains outright, lean on app-catalog and DLP tools that inspect known apps, or write policy no one enforces.
  • Why that fails. Blocking pushes people to personal devices, so shadow AI gets worse, not better. Catalogs lag the flood of new AI apps. Nothing governs agent or MCP traffic.

What it costs. 47% of people using generative AI still do so through personal, unmanaged accounts, and the average organization now sees 223 incidents a month of sensitive data going to AI apps, a year-over-year doubling (Netskope via Cybersecurity Dive, 2026). And the traffic is about to compound: Gartner forecasts 40% of enterprise apps will carry task-specific AI agents by end of 2026, up from under 5% in 2025, roughly an 8x jump in a year (Gartner via UC Today, 2025).

This is why it is an early-stage problem space. The category is being written now: Microsoft shipped Shadow AI detection and prompt-injection protection to general availability on March 31, 2026 (SiliconANGLE, 2026), and every major rival shipped comparable capabilities in the same window.

03Who it is for

I read the admin's real job as saying yes to AI safely, not blocking it. That reframe drives every decision below.

The security admin's job to be doneWhen employees and agents start using AI tools I cannot see, I want to discover what is in use and safely allow the good while stopping the risky, so I can enable AI without becoming the department of no.

Two jobs sit underneath that one: stop feeling blindsided and feel in control of a fast-moving risk, and be seen by the board as the team that enabled AI safely, not the team that blocked it.

04Why this problem

Of the three pillars this role could own, I would lead with safe access, because it is the highest-confidence start, then earn the harder two off it.

The role names three problem clusters. Reach is a relative read pending the real tenant baseline; impact and confidence are judgment tiers; effort is rough person-cost.

Pillar 1 already shipped, reaches everyone, and rides existing gateway plumbing, so it is the fastest, safest place to create pull. Pillar 2 stops the incident customers actually fear and raises what they will pay, but inspecting prompt content carries privacy and latency cost, so it needs the trust pillar 1 builds first. Pillar 3 is where the market is heading and where identity compounds, but it depends on another team's roadmap. Entra Agent ID, the identity layer that gives each agent an object ID (Microsoft Learn, Agent identities) and applies Conditional Access to it, reached GA in 2026 (Microsoft Learn, Agent ID). Pillar 3 lives or dies on interlocking with it, which is why I rank it a dependency-heavy bet, not a near-term ship. Sequence: land 1, attach 2, build toward 3.

05Approach

I would carry three bets forward and let customer evidence decide the order, not pick a favorite on day one.

Bet A

The discovery-to-policy loop

Make Shadow AI discovery flow straight into a one-click governed-allow policy. Closing the see-to-govern gap is what keeps admins using the feature.

Highest confidence, lowest effort. Leads.

Bet B

The data-aware gateway

Tie the access decision to data sensitivity using Microsoft Purview labels, so the same prompt is allowed or blocked based on the data it carries. Differentiates on an asset pure-network rivals do not own.

Higher effort, higher willingness to pay.

Bet C

Agent traffic control

Interlock Internet Access enforcement with Entra Agent ID so agent and MCP traffic is governed like human traffic. This is where the volume is going and where identity compounds.

Highest effort, cross-team, longest horizon.

I would score each against real customer evidence and the RICE read, then sequence by confidence and dependency: A now, because it is shippable; B next, because it lifts price and defensibility; C later, the compounding play I would de-risk early but ship after. The loop below is the mechanism Bet A turns on.

06What I'd ship first

The first thing I would ship is the shortest path from a discovery finding to a governed allow, tested on a handful of pilot tenants.

The MVP

From the Shadow AI discovery dashboard, let an admin pick a discovered AI app and apply a templated governed-allow policy in one flow: allow the sanctioned app, route it through inspection, block the personal-account variant, and log it back to the usage view. Ship it with templates for the top 20 AI services by observed usage.

The biggest assumption it tests

That security teams prefer governed-allow over block, and will turn it on and leave it on. Everything in the thesis rests on this. If pilot tenants flip from block to governed-allow and keep it enabled, the bet holds.

Why this first

It rides the already-shipped detection surface (low effort), reaches every tenant (high reach), and directly tests the load-bearing assumption. Detection already exists; the unproven part is whether the allow motion changes behavior.

What it deliberately leaves out

Full TLS and deep-packet inspection of prompt content at scale, agent and MCP enforcement, and custom policy authoring. Those wait until the loop earns trust.

07Metrics

I would measure whether customers are safely using AI, not how much AI we blocked.

North Star: monthly governed AI sessions across active tenants. A governed AI session is one flowing through an active Internet Access AI-control policy, allowed and inspected, not merely blocked. It rises only when a customer genuinely adopted safe AI, it moves before renewal and attach revenue, and the discovery-to-policy loop directly influences it.

Guardrails

Gateway p95 latency and availability, because this is an inline proxy for a customer's entire internet and AI traffic, and a bad rule can break legitimate AI access, not just miss a bad one. Microsoft's own dogfood rollout hit 150,000 users and 250,000 devices with expected latency cuts of 70 to 80% versus the legacy setup (Microsoft, 2025), which sets the reliability bar this work has to clear. The second guardrail is false-positive rate on detection and prompt-injection blocking, because over-blocking is how admins lose trust and switch the feature off.

Anti-metric: raw count of AI apps blocked. It looks like security but often means users moved to personal devices, so value dropped while the number rose. I would not let the team chase it.

08Business impact

I see the revenue lever here as attach on a base Microsoft already owns, not new logos.

Internet Access sells as a $5.00 per user per month standalone product and inside the Entra Suite at $12.00 per user per month (Microsoft Entra pricing). Unlike Zscaler, Netskope, Palo Alto, and Cloudflare, which have to win the network layer as a standalone sale, this capability is a checkbox add-on for an installed base measured in one billion identities and 1.6 million security customers (Microsoft, 2026). That is the distribution advantage.

  • Every incremental point of AI-control attach on the existing base is recurring per-user revenue at $5 to $12 per user per month, without a new sales motion. The discovery-to-governed-allow loop is designed to move exactly that number.
  • Pillar 2, data-aware protection, is the willingness-to-pay lever: it is the capability that can justify the Suite tier or a future add-on, because it stops the incident CISOs actually fear.
  • The whole bet sits inside a market Gartner sizes at $244.2 billion of information-security spend in 2026, up 13.3% year over year (Gartner via Software Strategies, 2026).

These are hypotheses tied to attach, not revenue promises. I would set the attach baseline in the first 90 days before committing to a target.

0930 / 60 / 90

My first quarter would be mostly listening, then one quick win, then one validated bet.

Days 0 to 30

Listen and learn the data path

Sit with the field, customer-engineering teams, and 8 to 10 enterprise security admins piloting Shadow AI discovery, and watch where they stall going from a finding to a policy. Go deep with engineering and SRE on the inline path: latency budget, failure modes, and how a bad rule breaks legitimate traffic. Map the four-team dependency graph. Pull the baseline: attach, false-positive rate, latency against SLO.

Days 30 to 60

One quick win, sharpen the problem

Ship the quick win: cut the clicks from a discovered risky AI app to an applied governed-allow policy for the top AI services. Low effort, extends the shipped surface, tests the core bet early. Turn the pilot interviews into clear scenarios, requirements, and success metrics. Align the four teams on the discovery, then data-label, then agent sequencing.

Days 60 to 90

Ship and validate the first bet

Run the governed-allow loop with 3 to 5 pilot tenants. Set the North Star baseline and the guardrails, latency and false-positive rate. Bring a data-aware policy prototype, a Purview label tied into allow-or-block, to the review board as the next differentiator bet. Propose the two-quarter roadmap sequencing pillars 1, then 2, then 3.

11Questions

A handful of answers would reshape this plan, so I would ask for them early.

  1. Which of the three pillars is the primary charter for this role right now, and what does leadership count as winning in 12 months: attach percentage, competitive parity, or a specific customer segment?
  2. What is the current attach rate and retention on Shadow AI discovery, and where do the latency and false-positive numbers sit today?
  3. Are the AI-security capabilities staying inside the $5 and $12 per-user tiers, or becoming a paid add-on? That changes the whole GTM.
  4. How firm is the Entra Agent ID integration contract and timeline for agent-traffic control?
  5. Where are we losing deals to Zscaler, Netskope, or Palo Alto today, and on which capability?
