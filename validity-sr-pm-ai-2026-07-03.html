The bet

Here's my bet: Validity's next agent should live on the CRM data-quality side, not on more email agents.

I'd build a "clean data for AI agents" layer on DemandTools and GridBuddy: an agent that tells a customer whether their CRM data is safe for AI to act on, and fixes what is not. Validity already sells the leading Salesforce data-quality tool, and it has shipped agents once, with Engage. Connecting those two is white space Validity owns the tools for, and it answers the loudest pain in the market: roughly 88% of enterprise AI agent pilots fail to reach production because of bad CRM data underneath them, not weak agents. It is the fastest route to the 12-month bar of moving the needle, through expansion and retention in the data-quality line.

The problem

My read of the portfolio: Validity shipped agents on the email side, but the CRM data underneath them is still nobody's product.

In February 2026 Validity shipped Engage: four AI agents (Ignite, Guardian, Expression, Insight) trained on its sender-reputation network of more than 2.5 billion data points a day. Every one of those agents sits on email and deliverability. DemandTools, GridBuddy, and BriteVerify have no public agent layer yet. That is the gap.

Who hurts, and what it costs

  • Who. RevOps and Salesforce admins who keep the CRM clean, and the GTM leaders above them who are now being told to deploy AI SDRs and agents on top of that same CRM.
  • The root cause. CRM contact data decays fast: B2B contact data goes stale at about 22.5% a year, and 91% of CRM data decays within a year. AI acts on whatever it finds, so a duplicate or a dead contact becomes an action, not just a bad row.
  • The workaround, and why it fails. Teams run periodic cleanup in tools like DemandTools. That was fine when a human read the list before acting. It fails the moment an autonomous agent acts on the data between cleanups, at machine speed, with no human in the loop.
  • The cost. Forrester projects more than $10 billion in 2026 losses tied to ungoverned AI in B2B sales and marketing. Validity's own April 2026 survey of 500+ marketers ranked poor data quality and proving AI's ROI as the #3 and #4 blockers to AI adoption, with only 12% of organizations at integrated AI maturity.

Competitors already see it. CRM data-quality vendors like Cloudingo, RingLead, and Insycle, and newer entrants like Tofu and Syncari, are repositioning around making CRM data safe for AI agents. That repositioning is moving faster than Validity's public story for DemandTools and GridBuddy. Closing that gap is the job.

Who it is for

Let me name the people, because the buyer is not the user here.

  • Primary user: the RevOps or Salesforce admin who runs DemandTools and GridBuddy today. They dedupe, standardize fields, and mass-edit records so downstream teams trust the CRM.
  • Economic buyer: the VP of RevOps, Marketing Ops, or the CRO who signs the expansion. They care about ROI they can show. Advanced AI adopters in Validity's survey were 75% more likely to hit ROI above 45 to 1, so the buyer wants that number to point at.
  • Gatekeeper: IT, security, and data governance. Their veto is simple: they will not let an agent write to the system of record unsupervised.

The job the admin is hiring this product to do

RevOps / Salesforce admin · primary user

When I am about to point AI agents or an AI SDR at our CRM, I want to know the data underneath will not make them act on duplicates or dead contacts, so I can turn the automation on without being blamed for what it does.

The job is also emotional and social. The emotional job: not be the person who gets blamed when the AI emails a dead lead. The social job is newer and stronger: be seen as the person who made the CRM AI-ready, a mandate RevOps leaders are being handed for the first time this year.

Approach

I see two directions worth running now, and a third to hold for later. Each is a bet I'd validate, not an answer.

Direction A: the readiness scan (read-only)

An agent that scores a CRM org's data health specifically for agent safety: duplicates, decayed contacts, missing fields the agents rely on. It explains each risk in plain language and recommends a fix, but changes nothing. Read-only clears the IT and governance gatekeeper on day one. This is the cheapest test of the biggest assumption.

Direction B: agentic cleanup

The same agent proposes fixes and, with a human's approval, executes them through DemandTools' existing operations. Higher value, higher trust bar. It only earns its place after A proves customers act on the findings.

Direction C: a cross-portfolio data-trust layer (later)

One layer that feeds both sides: BriteVerify-verified emails and DemandTools-clean records, so the deliverability agents and the CRM agents draw on the same trusted data. Bigger, and it should wait until the wedge is real.

How I'd decide between them: prototype A, put it in front of design partners, and watch whether the scan output actually changes what they do. If it does not change behavior, B and C are premature no matter how good the demo looks.

What I'd ship first

I'd ship the smallest safe thing: a read-only scan that tests the biggest assumption.

A read-only AI data-readiness scan on a design partner's Salesforce sandbox, built in Python against the Salesforce API with an LLM to explain findings. It is the smallest test of the biggest assumption: that RevOps leaders treat "agent-ready data" as a distinct, budgeted problem, not re-badged cleanup.

Why this first, and what it leaves out. By RICE: high reach (most of the DemandTools and GridBuddy base faces the AI-readiness question this year), impact 3 (it enables the customer's own AI plans), confidence 80%, and low-to-medium effort because it reuses the checks DemandTools already runs. It deliberately leaves out writing any change back to the CRM, multi-agent orchestration, and anything cross-portfolio. All of that waits until the scan proves customers act on it. This also hits the 90-day bar: a working prototype that shows a meaningful capability, not a slide.

Metrics

This is how I'd know it's working.

The North Star for the product line is weekly active accounts that apply at least one recommended fix. If it rises, a customer's CRM genuinely got safer for their AI; it moves before renewal revenue does; and the team can influence it through better detection and clearer recommendations. For the read-only MVP, the leading proxy is narrower: accounts that run a scan and change something as a result.

The anti-metrics matter as much as the North Star. I would not chase raw edits made: an agent that mass-edits looks busy while quietly corrupting the system of record, the exact failure this product exists to prevent. I would not chase scans run, a vanity count. And I would watch false-positive flags closely, because an agent that cries wolf on clean records gets switched off faster than one that misses a few.

30 / 60 / 90

My first ninety days: listen and map, prototype, then propose the roadmap.

0 to 30 days · Listen and map

Find where cleanup clusters

Pull DemandTools and GridBuddy usage data to find where manual cleanup clusters. Interview RevOps admins and the Engage team about which agents customers actually adopted versus which just demoed well. Map Engage's existing agent, eval, and guardrail architecture so I reuse it instead of rebuilding it. Line up three to five design partners. Stand up a throwaway prototype against a sample Salesforce org to learn the data shape.

30 to 60 days · Sharpen and prototype

Ship the read-only scan to design partners

Ship the first working read-only readiness scan into two or three design-partner sandboxes. Measure whether the output changes their behavior, asking what they did the last time an AI tool acted on bad CRM data, not whether they would like this. Align engineering, the Engage team, and RevOps leadership on the wedge.

60 to 90 days · Harden and propose

Pilot, baseline, propose the roadmap

Turn the prototype into a customer-facing pilot with explicit success and kill criteria. Set the metric baseline (data health score, recommendation acceptance). Propose the roadmap from scan, to approved-fix, to the cross-portfolio data-trust layer. This clears the 90-day bar.

Risks

What I'm not sure of yet, and how I'd test it in week one. Every one is about the work, not a settled fact.

The demand assumption

Do RevOps leaders treat "agent-ready data" as a distinct, budgeted problem, or just re-badged cleanup? This is the whole thesis. Validate: customer interviews about past behavior, first.

The governance veto

IT may refuse to let any agent write to the system of record. Mitigate: ship read-only first, so the wedge does not depend on write access.

Architecture reuse

Engage's agent framework was built for email data. It may not fit CRM data shapes or Salesforce API limits. Confirm: map that architecture in month one before committing to reuse.

False positives

In data quality, crying wolf on clean records kills trust faster than missing a risk. Test: accuracy against a labeled review set before any customer sees output.

Positioning overlap

"AI-readiness" could blur into BriteVerify and Engage in the customer's mind. Sequence: the portfolio story has to be crisp before launch, not after.

Questions

Five answers that would change my plan.

  1. Does this role own AI across the whole portfolio, or is it scoped to the Engage and Litmus side first? The JD says "across its products," but Engage is the only shipped agentic product so far.
  2. What did Engage teach us about which agents customers adopt and keep, versus which demo well and go unused?
  3. Is the current agent, eval, and guardrail stack meant to be portfolio-wide infrastructure, or was it built specifically for Engage?
  4. What data access and API limits do we have into a customer's Salesforce or Dynamics org for a data-quality agent to read?
  5. How is "moving the needle" actually measured at 12 months, expansion revenue, retention, or usage, and which product line's number matters most?
