The bet

Here's my bet: the scarce skill in this role is not shipping another enterprise chatbot. It is making internal AI provably worth it and defensible to a regulator, so the tools survive past pilot.

I would attack the roughly 95% pilot-stall rate (MIT, via Fortune 2025) by standing up a governance and ROI scorecard first, then shipping automation that can pass it. The measure I care about is the count of internal AI initiatives that are in real use, ROI-proven, and cleared on model risk, not the count of pilots launched.

The problem

My read: BlackRock does not have too little internal automation. It has the opposite risk, too many ungoverned AI tools that never prove their worth.

The human version: an ops analyst still cannot get a fast, trustworthy answer, and the leader who funded the tool still cannot prove it earned its keep. Everyone is busy; nobody can point to the win.

  • Who hurts. Internal teams (ops, infrastructure, compliance) who need fast, trustworthy answers to do their jobs. The Technology Product leaders who fund these tools and now have to defend them. And the PM who owns the roadmap across 10+ engineering teams without managing any of them.
  • What breaks. AI tools get built and launched, then stall before they show measurable impact or a defensible risk story.
  • Root cause. Internal builds succeed only about a third as often as vendor-bought or partner-built tools (MIT, via Fortune 2025). Each app gets its own AI bolted on: "Agents are deployed without governance, and intelligence is disconnected from execution" (ServiceNow 2026). And the regulatory bar just moved: the Federal Reserve's April 2026 model-risk rule (SR 26-2, replacing a 15-year-old standard) applies to banks with over $30 billion in assets (Federal Reserve 2026).
  • How people cope, and why it fails. The default is to launch more pilots and add more agents. Enterprises now run about 12 AI agents each, headed to 20, and more than 4 in 5 IT leaders say the sprawl itself creates more complexity than value (Salesforce Connectivity Benchmark, via CIO Dive 2026). Gartner expects over 40% of agentic AI projects to be canceled by end of 2027 on cost, unclear value, or weak risk controls (Gartner, via PAW 2025).
  • What it costs. Killed initiatives, wasted engineering spend, and regulatory exposure. This is board-visible: BlackRock's technology services and subscription revenue grew 22% year over year to $530 million last quarter, against $13.9 trillion in AUM (Q1 2026 filing, via StockTitan). When technology is a headline number every quarter, an internal-AI program that cannot show a return is a program that gets cut.

Who it is for

My read: this is a two-sided internal problem. The daily user wants a trustworthy answer. The funder wants proof. The tool has to serve both or it dies.

Primary user · internal knowledge worker (ops or infrastructure analyst)

"When I hit a question I cannot answer from memory (a policy, an approval path, which system owns a process), I want a correct answer with its source, so I can act without waiting on a person or risking a compliance miss."

Economic buyer · Technology Product leader who funds the initiative

"When I fund an AI tool, I want proof it moved a number and will not create regulatory exposure, so I can defend it on an earnings-relevant technology line."

The knowledge worker fires the current fix (Slack a colleague, dig through a wiki, file a ticket) the moment something answers faster and can be trusted. Trust is the switching cost, not speed.

  • Gatekeeper: compliance and model risk. Under SR 26-2, this function can veto. A tool that touches a regulated decision without a model-risk record does not ship. The buyer's job has an emotional layer too: they want to fund AI without being the person who shipped the ungoverned agent a regulator later flags.
  • The 10+ engineering leads who build. Their job is social: a clear operating model so AI work is planned capacity, not unplanned risk.

Why this problem

My read: of everything this role could fund, the highest-leverage move is the layer every AI initiative has to pass through, not any single new tool.

Here is the reasoning, scored on the same scales. Reach is internal AI initiatives touched per quarter (using the industry figure of ~12 live agents per enterprise as a stand-in), impact on a 0.25 to 3 scale, effort in person-months (people times months).

RACI means naming, for each step, who is responsible, accountable, consulted, and informed. Two things fall out. Building one more chatbot before the evidence layer exists scores near zero, because it touches one initiative and carries the same 50/50 odds of stalling as the industry base rate. The operating model scores highest because it is cheap and unblocks every team. The governance and ROI scorecard and the model-risk intake are the load every tool has to carry, so they stay in the first cut regardless of raw score. They are not competing with the tools they enable.

Approach

My read: this is a build-versus-buy call before it is a build call, and the evidence says be careful about building.

  1. Build the control plane in-house. A BlackRock-owned registry plus governance and ROI scorecard. Most control, but internal builds carry the lower success rate above.
  2. Adopt a vendor control plane and wrap it with BlackRock policy. ServiceNow (named as a preferred skill in the JD) is positioning its AI Control Tower for exactly governed, observable agents. Buy the plumbing, build the model-risk policy and the executive scorecard on top.
  3. Federated. A thin central standard (one intake, one ROI scorecard, one model-risk template) with the 10+ teams self-serving against it. Lightest to stand up, weakest control until the standard has teeth.

How I would decide: run a real bake-off against BlackRock's own regulated criteria (capability, ingestion and query cost, groundedness, model-risk fit), not a slideware comparison. My prior leans toward direction 2 plus a federated standard: buy the governed-execution plumbing, own the policy and the leadership-facing scorecard, because the MIT data says the in-house build is where these efforts most often die.

Groundedness here means whether an answer is backed by a real source rather than made up.

What I'd ship first

My read: the smallest test of the biggest assumption, that a governed ROI scorecard actually changes a funding decision, and that a model-risk intake unblocks shipping rather than blocking it.

Ship: take one existing, high-traffic internal AI tool (an IT or ops helpdesk assistant is the likely candidate). Instrument it end to end: weekly active users, manual hours removed, deflection (share of questions answered without a human), and answer groundedness. Attach a one-page model-risk record that would stand up to SR 26-2 scrutiny. Put that single scorecard in front of the leaders who fund it.

Leave out, on purpose: firm-wide rollout, the full initiative registry, an automated intake, and the multi-team RACI. Those come after the scorecard proves it moves a decision.

Why this first: highest confidence per unit of effort. It reuses a tool that already exists, so effort is low, and it tests the one belief the whole thesis rests on before anyone funds a platform.

Metrics

My read: the honest North Star is not pilots launched, it is initiatives that are used, worth it, and defensible at once.

North Star: governed, in-production AI initiatives. The monthly count of internal AI initiatives that are (a) in active use past pilot, (b) carry a current, signed ROI record, and (c) carry a current model-risk sign-off. It passes the three tests: if it rises the firm genuinely won, it moves before the technology-services line does, and this role's operating model directly drives it.

Guardrails

  • Answer groundedness stays above an agreed bar, so we never buy adoption with a tool that makes things up.
  • Model-risk sign-off coverage stays at 100% for any tool touching a regulated decision.

Anti-metrics, named so no one chases them: number of pilots launched, number of agents deployed, raw message or session volume, and time spent in the tool. Each looks like progress while value leaks.

Operating model

Here's the loop the work creates: every initiative moves from intake, through a build-or-buy call and a model-risk record, out to users, and into a leadership scorecard that feeds the next funding decision.

30 / 60 / 90

My read: listen before I build anything, because the real constraints here are political and regulatory, not technical.

0 to 30 days · Listen

Meet the 10+ engineering leads, compliance and model risk, and the ops stakeholders. Inventory every live internal AI tool and pilot. Pull whatever adoption data already exists. Read the current model-risk process against SR 26-2 and find out what "FIPS-compliant" (a federal security standard) actually gates here. Map who can veto.

30 to 60 days · Quick win and sharpen

Ship the ROI and model-risk scorecard for one existing tool. Run discovery interviews to rank the real friction. Publish a thin operating model (one intake, one RACI) that the teams agree to rather than one I impose. Kick off the build-versus-buy bake-off.

60 to 90 days · Validate the first bet

Put the scorecard in front of a real funding decision and watch whether it changes the call. Set the North Star baseline. Propose the governed-adoption roadmap and the platform recommendation.

Risks

My read: the thesis rests on four assumptions about the work, and each is testable early.

Leaders may not change a decision just because they have a scorecard.

This is the core assumption. Validate: run the MVP scorecard against one live funding decision in the first weeks. If it does not move the call, the metric is wrong and I rebuild it.

A model-risk intake could become the bottleneck it was meant to prevent.

Governance that slows shipping kills adoption. Validate: time-box the intake, measure its cycle time as a first-class metric, and co-design it with compliance so it clears tools rather than parking them.

Buying the control plane may not fit BlackRock's regulated constraints.

Validate: a real bake-off scored on model-risk fit and cost against actual internal policy documents, not a vendor demo.

The operating model can be ignored, since roadmap authority is not org authority over the 10+ teams.

Validate: co-author the RACI with the engineering leads and check whether the intake is actually used within 30 days. Adoption of the process is the leading signal that the rest will work.

Questions

Here are the answers that would change my plan.

  1. Does this role own the build-versus-buy decision on the control plane, or is a vendor already chosen?
  2. Where does "AI and Enterprise Platforms" end and the Digital Solutions org (Copilot, ChatGPT, collaboration tooling) begin? Who owns the employee-facing assistant?
  3. Under SR 26-2, which internal AI tools are already in scope as "models," and who signs off today?
  4. What does the funder count as success first: hours saved, adoption, or a defensible risk story?
  5. Do the 10+ engineering teams share one definition of "shipped," or does each define done differently?
