The bet

My bet: this team compounds value fastest when it is graded on demand served per unit of engineering effort, not on how many agents it builds.

I would run a disciplined front door that routes each request to the cheapest durable answer: self-serve guidance, a shared catalog agent, or a new managed build. Every managed build is a candidate to become reusable platform capability. Done well, the same engineering time serves more demand each quarter while the raw count of one-off managed agents falls by design. That is how a team whose success metric is its own shrinking dependency stays motivated and stays funded.

Managed agent
A centrally built, production-grade AI agent maintained by WPP's engineering team.
Self-serve
The requester builds it themselves in the no-code Agent Builder.
Transition
Moving a managed agent into a self-serve feature, into a shared catalog agent, or confirming it stays bespoke.

The problem

The tension I would be hired to manage is unusual, and it sits at the center of everything below.

The public story is that WPP Open democratizes expertise and anyone can build agents with no code. The operational reality is that a large, engineering-heavy layer still sits underneath that story, and someone has to decide, case by case, what can and cannot be self-served yet. This role owns that decision, and it is built to make itself less necessary over time.

  • Who hurts. Agency and client teams with a recurring, high-stakes task their own no-code agents cannot do reliably. They need something they can trust in front of a client without babysitting the output.
  • Why now. WPP Open already carries over 75,000 users and more than 90% of client-facing staff (WPP, Jan 2026). Over the prior year, staff and clients built tens of thousands of bespoke agents, and the curated Agent Hub launched with around 150 vetted ready-made agents plus four flagship Super Agents (Campaign, Jan 2026). Demand has outrun the ability to serve it centrally.
  • The workaround, and why it fails. Today the default answer to a hard request is "build another managed agent." That does not scale, it duplicates work across overlapping bespoke agents, and it grows a backlog the team can never fully clear.
  • What it costs. WPP is under real cost pressure. Full-year 2025 revenue fell to £13,550m, down 8.1% reported (WPP, Feb 2026), and Q1 2026 fell a further 6.6% reported (WPP, Apr 2026). Every hour of scarce specialist engineering time spent on a one-off build is an hour not spent making self-serve better.
  • The industry backdrop. Gartner projects the average Fortune 500 company will run over 150,000 agents by 2028, up from about 15 in 2025, and that only 13% of organizations believe their agent governance is adequate today (Gartner via Tech.co, Apr 2026). Agent count is exploding ahead of governance everywhere, which is exactly the gap this role and Agent Governance Operations are split to cover.
One thing to confirm first The role's scope references a 150,000-plus agent estate. Publicly, WPP has confirmed about 150 curated ready-made agents plus tens of thousands of bespoke agents. The larger figure may be an internal inventory count or a forward target. I would confirm the real, queryable inventory before I bet a mining strategy on it.

Who it's for

I see four groups feeling this problem, and each is hiring this capability to do a different job.

The engineering team (ex-Satalia specialists) Internal builders When I invest months in a hard agent, show me it mattered and will not be quietly killed, so I stay motivated on a team whose mandate is to shrink.

The requester is the user. The executive is the buyer. The engineering team is the group whose trust I most have to earn, because the metric that defines success can read as a threat to them if I frame it badly. More on that under metrics.

  • The scored intake wins by a wide margin. It touches every incoming request, it is cheap because it is mostly a scoring rubric and a decision record rather than new tooling, and it is the single lever that sets the managed-versus-self-serve split. The whole thesis rides on it, so I fund it first.
  • The quality bar comes second because it unlocks confident deflection. I can only route demand away from a managed build if requesters trust the alternative.   • Insight-mining scores low on confidence on purpose. Its value depends on whether the agent estate is actually queryable at scale. Until I have proven the data is real and structured, I mine by hand.
  • The transition engine is the strategic payoff but is sequenced last. It is high-effort and depends on the intake and quality bar existing first. It is where managed builds convert into durable capability, so it is last in order, not in importance.

Approach

I would hold three directions open and let evidence pick, rather than committing to one on day one.

Direction A

A scored front door

Route every request through validation, feasibility, and a risk-and-reuse score that pushes it to self-serve, catalog, or managed.

Bet: most requests do not actually need a bespoke build. Test by scoring real requests and measuring how many stick as self-serve.

Direction B

A quality bar that makes deflection safe

Define production-grade once, apply it to every managed agent, and use it as the standard self-serve agents aspire to.

Bet: trust, not capability, is what blocks self-serve adoption. Test by tracking whether deflected requesters succeed unaided.

Direction C

A transition pipeline

Assign every managed agent a fate and actually move the good ones into self-serve features or catalog agents.

Bet: managed builds are reusable more often than they look. Test by promoting one or two live agents and measuring reuse.

These are not exclusive. My read is that A comes first and unblocks the rest, B runs alongside it to make routing safe, and C follows once there is a stocked managed fleet worth transitioning. The intake flow I would run:

What I'd ship first

I would start with the smallest thing that tests the biggest assumption.

That assumption is that demand can actually be routed away from managed builds and that self-serve absorbs it. If it cannot, the declining-dependency thesis collapses, so I want to know early. The MVP, run over the first 60 to 90 days:

  • A scored intake rubric plus a written decision record, applied live to the next quarter's incoming requests. Each request gets a managed, self-serve, or feature decision and a one-line reason.
  • A first-draft production-grade checklist, tested against two or three real managed agents, so the quality bar is grounded in live cases, not written in the abstract.
  • A top-patterns memo to the two sibling PMs, listing the most common recurring needs I see coming through the door.

What it deliberately leaves out: automated dedup tooling, live monitoring dashboards, and the full transition pipeline. Those are worth building only after the intake data proves the routing works and the estate proves minable.

What the MVP produces is a baseline I do not have today: the real managed-versus-self-serve split, a deflection rate, and, most important, a "deflection that stuck" number, meaning requests routed to self-serve that did not bounce back within 90 days.

Metrics

I would grade the team on leverage, not output.

The JD's stated metric is decreasing dependency on managed agents. A raw shrink number is easy to game, just reject requests, and it quietly tells the engineering team their job is to disappear. So I would make the North Star a value metric and keep the shrink signal as a guardrail.

North Star: recurring, validated demand met by durable capability, per unit of managed-engineering effort.

As self-serve matures, the same effort serves more demand, because managed builds get promoted into reusable features and catalog agents instead of staying one-off. The number rises even as raw managed-build count falls. That reframe is what keeps the team motivated: they are building leverage, not building themselves out of a job.

The guardrails matter as much as the North Star. Quality must not slip as I push volume toward self-serve. Decision speed must stay fast so the intake does not become a bottleneck. And the bounce-back anti-metric stops me from claiming a win for deflections that quietly fail and return.

Business impact

I would tie this work directly to the cost mandate WPP just set itself.

The Elevate28 plan restructures WPP into four operating units unified by WPP Open and targets £500m of annualized cost savings by 2028 (WPP, Feb 2026), against the revenue decline noted above. Framed as hypotheses, not promises:

  • Managed agents that graduate into reusable self-serve or catalog capability lower the marginal cost of serving recurring demand. One build serves many teams instead of one. That is the efficiency lever an Elevate28-era platform needs.
  • Disciplined intake protects scarce specialist engineering time by keeping it on the hard, genuinely bespoke work and off duplicate builds. Under cost pressure, protecting that time is a defensible commercial argument for keeping the team funded.
  • The insight signals fed to sibling PMs reduce wasted roadmap effort across Agent Hub and Agent Builder, so the platform invests ahead of demand instead of behind it.

I would want to attach real numbers to each of these once I can see intake volume and per-build cost, which is why they sit in the first-90-days plan rather than as claims here.

The first 90 days

I would spend the first month listening before I change any process.

Days 0 to 30

Listen and baseline

Read every intake case from the last two quarters. Sit with the ex-Satalia engineering team and understand what they are proud of and what they dread. Meet the two sibling PMs and Agent Governance Operations to map the current handoffs. Pull the backlog and measure today's real managed-versus-self-serve split. Talk to a handful of requesters. Confirm what quality standards exist and how the estate inventory is stored.

Days 30 to 60

A quick win and a sharper problem

Ship the scored intake rubric and the first-draft quality checklist, and run them live on incoming requests. Publish a baseline deflection dashboard. Send the first top-patterns memo to the sibling PMs. Co-design the North Star with the engineering team so they own the framing.

Days 60 to 90

Validate the first bet and propose the roadmap

Assign a transition fate to the top managed agents and move one or two into self-serve or catalog as a proof. Set the metric baselines. Propose the portfolio roadmap and a standing operating rhythm for intake, quality, and transition.

Risks and what I'd validate first

The thesis rests on a few assumptions about the work, and I would test each in week one rather than assume it.

  • Self-serve is mature enough to absorb deflected demand. If the Agent Builder cannot yet do what requesters need, disciplined intake just creates frustration. Validate by running five real deflected cases through self-serve and watching whether the requester succeeds unaided.
  • The agent estate is queryable at scale. My insight-mining plan depends on it, and the public numbers do not confirm a 150,000-plus minable inventory. Validate by auditing the actual inventory and its structure before building any mining tooling.
  • A shrink metric will not demoralize the engineering team. Validate by co-designing the North Star with them as a leverage metric, not a headcount metric, and checking that it reads that way to them.
  • The quality bar and Agent Governance Operations do not overlap or conflict. Validate by mapping both in week one and agreeing who owns the accuracy threshold.
  • Intake discipline could become a bottleneck. A slow intake process loses trust fast. Validate against the cycle-time guardrail and cap decision turnaround from the start.

Questions I'd ask the team

I would want answers to a handful of questions before I commit to this plan.

  1. Of the last quarter's requests, how many became managed builds versus self-serve versus feature requests, and who made each call today?
  2. How queryable is the agent estate right now? Can I actually mine tens of thousands of bespoke agents for patterns, or is that inventory not yet structured for it?
  3. Where does the managed-agents quality bar end and Agent Governance Operations verification begin? Who owns the accuracy threshold?
  4. What does decreasing dependency mean to leadership in numbers, and over what horizon, given the Elevate28 cost targets?
  5. When a managed agent graduates to a self-serve feature, who carries the maintenance and the client relationship afterward? What is the handoff contract with the sibling PMs?
