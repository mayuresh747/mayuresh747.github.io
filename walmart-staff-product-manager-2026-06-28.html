The bet

Walmart already proved AI can negotiate. The open question is how much of the supplier base it can reach, and how fast.

The autonomous negotiation bot closed deals with 68% of suppliers and saved about 3% on the spend it touched (PYMNTS / Bloomberg, 2023). So the bet now is not "can AI negotiate." It is reach: how much of the supplier base gets touched at all.

I would aim the team at one number: the share of supplier spend that gets an active, data-backed negotiation each year. Closing the long tail and arming merchants with negotiation intelligence is where the next large cost savings sit.

The problem

Most of Walmart's suppliers never get a real negotiation, because a buyer's time costs more than a small deal is worth.

Walmart buys from more than 100,000 suppliers (HBR, 2022). Human buyers cannot reach most of them, so roughly 20% sit on standard "cookie-cutter" terms that are often not negotiated (HBR, 2022). That is money left on the table, every year, by design.

  • Who hurts. Walmart merchants, the buyers and category managers who own supplier relationships and cost targets. They are time-poor and data-hungry, and the long tail never gets their attention.
  • What it costs. At $706.41 billion in FY2026 net sales (Yahoo Finance, reporting Walmart, 2026), even a one-tenth of one percent improvement in cost of goods is enormous. The tail and the unnegotiated terms are real, recoverable dollars.
  • Root cause. Negotiation is gated by human capacity. A buyer can only run so many deals at once, so low-value contracts never get touched.
  • Workarounds today. Templated terms for the tail, plus the new GenAI assistant (Wally) that frees merchant time by automating data work (Walmart, March 2025). The autonomous bot handles a slice of the tail.
  • Why they fall short. The bot has been confirmed mostly on indirect spend (fleet services, shopping carts), not goods for resale (Talking Logistics, 2023). Wally analyzes but does not drive negotiations. The wide middle, deciding which contracts to renegotiate now and giving merchants the leverage to win, is open.
There is a clock on this

In 2025, as tariffs loomed, Walmart asked suppliers for price cuts of up to 10% per round (PYMNTS, 2025). That is exactly the high-frequency, many-supplier renegotiation human teams cannot do at speed. A tool that can find exposed contracts and act fast becomes a margin lever, not a nice-to-have.

Who it is for

JTBD = the real outcome a person hires the product to achieve, stated as a need, not a feature.

Primary user: the merchant / buyer

When a new cost or term opportunity appears across my supplier base, I want to know which deals are worth pursuing and walk in with the data to win them, so I spend my limited time on the negotiations that move my margin, not on chasing reports.

The economic decision-maker: merchandising leadership

They fund the team and own the cost-of-goods number. Their job is to hit margin targets across categories without breaking supplier relationships. They will judge this product on dollars saved, coverage expanded, and whether merchants actually trust and use it.

Indirect user: the supplier

Their experience shapes whether they respond and agree. The signal is good: 83% of suppliers found the AI system easy to use, and about 75% preferred negotiating with the bot over a person (PYMNTS / Bloomberg, 2023). Suppliers are not the obstacle. Reach and merchant trust are.

The read. The two highest-scoring bets are expanding tail automation and building the negotiation-intelligence layer. Tail expansion has the highest confidence because the engine is proven. Intelligence has the broadest reach because it serves every merchant on every deal, and it is the foundation the tariff workflow and direct-merchandise negotiation both need. I would fund those two first and treat direct merchandise as the prize we sequence toward, once trust and data quality are established.

Approach

Three hypotheses. I would run all three as small tests early, then concentrate effort where the evidence is strongest.

  • Reach: widen autonomous negotiation across the tail. The proven engine can extend to new categories and the next supplier tier with configurable guardrails, and savings will hold. Decide by piloting one or two new categories and watching close rate, savings, and supplier acceptance against the established benchmarks.
  • Leverage: a negotiation-intelligence layer for merchants. Merchants act faster and win more when the product ranks which contracts to renegotiate now and shows the supporting data (cost benchmarks, supplier performance, exposure). Decide by measuring whether ranked, evidence-backed recommendations change what merchants do and save, versus their current workflow.
  • Speed: a cost-shock response workflow. When costs move (tariffs, commodities), merchants need a fast path from "which contracts are exposed" to "renegotiate these now." Decide by running it against a real cost event and measuring time from signal to action.
How I would choose

Data readiness and merchant trust are the gates. Where the data is clean and merchants trust the recommendation, autonomous action wins. Where data is messy or stakes are high, the product recommends and the merchant decides. It should move along that line as confidence is earned, not jump to full autonomy everywhere.

What I'd ship first

A negotiation-intelligence shortlist for one merchant team, in one category.

The smallest thing that tests the biggest assumption. It ranks the merchant's contracts by renegotiation opportunity (size of spend, terms gap, recent cost movement) and shows the data behind each one. The merchant picks targets and acts. For clearly tail-end, low-stakes contracts, it offers a one-click handoff to the existing autonomous engine.

The assumption it tests

That merchants will trust and act on a ranked, evidence-backed list, and that doing so produces measurable savings beyond their current workflow. If that holds, the path to scale and more autonomy is clear. If it does not, I learn it cheaply, in one category, before committing engineering to a broad build.

Why this first

High reach (it serves the merchant's whole book in that category), high impact (it points effort at the best deals), and the highest confidence available, because it leans on data the team already assembles for Wally and an engine that already negotiates. Effort is contained to one team and one category.

What it deliberately leaves out

Broad rollout, direct-merchandise negotiation, multi-category coverage, and any fully autonomous action on high-stakes contracts. Those come after the shortlist earns trust and the savings are real.

Metrics

North Star

Negotiated coverage of supplier spend: the share of supplier spend that received an active, data-backed negotiation in the trailing year. It ties straight to cost of goods and captures the core problem, that most of the base is never reached. Every feature should move this.

Guardrails: the metrics that say "do not win the wrong way"

  • Merchant trust. If the override rate climbs or merchants stop coming back, the recommendations are wrong or untrusted. Coverage gained by ignoring merchants is fragile.
  • Supplier health. Watch supplier response and ease-of-use, already strong at 83% (PYMNTS / Bloomberg, 2023). Coverage that burns supplier goodwill is a loss.

Business impact

The proven savings rate is settled. The lever is the base: how much spend gets negotiated at all.

At $706.41 billion in FY2026 net sales (Yahoo Finance, reporting Walmart, 2026), small percentage moves on cost of goods are large absolute dollars. The proven savings rate on AI-negotiated spend is about 3% (PYMNTS / Bloomberg, 2023). Each percentage point of additional supplier spend brought into active, data-backed negotiation, at that savings rate, is a direct cost-of-goods improvement. That is the line the North Star tracks, and the case for funding reach over polish.

30 / 60 / 90

Days 0 to 30

Listen and find the real constraints

  • Sit with merchants across a few categories. Map where their time actually goes and where the tail is most under-negotiated.
  • Read the autonomous-engine and Wally data with the team: close rates, savings, where coverage stops and why.
  • Meet the partners (ML engineering, data platform, Legal, Finance, Global Sourcing) and learn what is built in-house versus licensed, since that sets what we own.
  • Find the data constraints early: how clean and how connected is cost, supplier-performance, and contract data.
Days 30 to 60

A quick win and a sharper problem

  • Ship the negotiation-intelligence shortlist to one merchant team in one category.
  • Baseline the North Star and the guardrails so we can prove movement, not assert it.
  • Align leadership on the coverage thesis and the sequence: tail and intelligence first, direct merchandise later.
Days 60 to 90

Validate the first bet and propose the roadmap

  • Measure the shortlist: did merchants act, did savings beat their old workflow, did trust hold.
  • Use the result to size the next bets and propose a multi-quarter roadmap from tail expansion, to intelligence at scale, to a cost-shock workflow, toward direct merchandise.
  • Set up the experimentation loop so every later feature is judged against coverage and the guardrails.

Risks

The risks in the proposed work, and how I would test each one early.

  • Data quality gates everything. Recommendations are only as good as the cost, supplier, and contract data behind them. Validate first: in week one, trace the data behind a handful of real contracts end to end and check it is accurate and current before any recommendation ships.
  • Merchant trust is fragile. A wrong or unexplained recommendation early can sour adoption for a long time. Validate first: ship the shortlist as a recommendation with the supporting data visible and an easy override, and watch override rate and repeat use as the real signal.
  • Direct merchandise is harder than indirect spend. The proven results are mostly indirect spend, not goods for resale. Validate first: prove the intelligence layer and tail expansion on lower-stakes spend before committing to autonomous action on direct merchandise.
  • Build versus buy is unsettled. It is not fully clear in public sources how much of the negotiation capability is in-house versus licensed, which changes what the team controls. Validate first: confirm ownership with engineering in the first weeks, since it shapes the roadmap.
  • Autonomy must be earned, not assumed. Pushing full autonomy onto high-stakes deals too early invites a costly mistake. Validate first: keep a human in the loop above a confidence and value threshold, and only widen autonomy where the data and outcomes justify it.

Questions

What I would ask the team in the first weeks.

  1. How is negotiated coverage measured today, if at all, and what share of supplier spend gets an active negotiation now? That sets the baseline for everything.
  2. Which parts of the negotiation stack are built on Walmart's own platform versus licensed from a vendor? That defines what we own and can move fastest on.
  3. Where does the autonomous engine stop today, by category, supplier tier, and direct versus indirect spend, and what blocked the next step?
  4. What does merchant adoption of the GenAI assistant actually look like in numbers, and where do merchants still drop back to manual work?
  5. When merchants distrust an AI recommendation, what is the specific reason most often: the data, the explanation, or the stakes? That tells me where to invest in trust.
