Why this role

Splitero built almost exactly the product I once wished existed.

About three years ago I bought a home in India. I remember wishing I could unlock some of its value without taking on another monthly loan payment, and picturing almost exactly what Splitero built: cash today for a share of the home's future value, with no monthly bill. Indian regulation did not allow anything like it then, so the idea stayed an idea. Watching Splitero make that real for homeowners across 14 states is what first pulled me toward the company.

What pulls me toward this specific role is that it sits behind the product, on the internal system that 140+ underwriting, servicing, funding, and compliance staff use to actually fund those homeowners (Splitero JD). That is the work I have done before. At Lentra I built the digital lending platform behind India's largest private bank, and my job was to take a slow, manual, multi-stage process and make it fast without breaking it.

The bet

My bet is that the fastest way to help Splitero scale is to make the platform show, in real time, where each application loses time, then remove that friction one stage at a time.

Splitero closed two rated securitizations in six months, $283.3 million in November 2025 (9.25x oversubscribed) and $296 million in May 2026. At a growth-stage, non-bank originator with that kind of capital behind it, the constraint is not demand. It is funding more contracts at close to the same headcount.

The problem

I'd frame the platform's real job as moving contracts through a slow, document-heavy pipeline without adding a person for every extra file.

  • Who hurts. The 140+ ops staff across underwriting, servicing, funding, and compliance run every contract through one Salesforce instance they did not choose. They were handed it.
  • What it is. A manual, judgment-heavy path: a fast online eligibility check, a full application, a human underwriting review that includes a property appraisal, then funding by wire. Public reviews put total time to funding at 14 to 30 days.
  • Root cause. The heavy steps are property valuation and human review, and every handoff between departments adds waiting time.
  • The workarounds today. First-come-first-served request queues, side spreadsheets, and "how we have always done it." These do not scale, and a mandated tool without genuine buy-in tends to breed shadow infrastructure and reduced trust.
  • What it costs. Slower funding, and higher error and rework risk at the exact moment regulators are tightening. Illinois adopted HEI-specific rules with a 36% APR cap effective June 1, 2026, Maine reclassified these products as consumer loans in April 2026, and a federal bill (S. 4803) would pull them under the Truth in Lending Act. Compliance now has to be accurate and auditable state by state.

Who it is for

I'd treat the customer here as internal: the daily users are the 140+ ops staff, and the buyers are the ops leads and the Director of Product who owns the platform outcome.

At a direct peer, Hometap, the equivalent leaders are measured on exactly this: reducing cycle time and improving team capacity, working with Product, Engineering, and Salesforce. Same shape of job.

Why this problem

If I were choosing where to start, I would rank the obvious platform bets before committing to any one of them.

The scores below are an outside-in read to be recalibrated with real data in week one, not a verdict. Reach is how many people or files a bet touches, impact is how much it moves cycle time or risk, confidence is how sure I am, and effort is rough build cost.

B. Structured intake with data-driven prioritization (replace first-come-first-served) All 4 departments + eng Medium-high High Low-medium Do early. The JD names it, and it makes prioritization transparent.

A and B are high confidence and lower effort, and they unlock everything else. C is likely the biggest single win on cycle time, but I would not build it until the instrumentation proves the valuation step really is where time is lost. That is why A comes first.

Approach

I would resist jumping to a solution and instead let three directions compete on evidence.

  • Direction 1 · instrument firstInstrument before I change anything. Make each stage's cycle time, error and rework rate, and request turnaround visible first. You cannot cut a bottleneck you cannot see.
  • Direction 2 · structured intakeMake structured intake the operating rhythm. One front door for requests across departments, scored against impact, instead of whoever asks loudest going first.
  • Direction 3 · config vs buildGovern config versus build deliberately. Default to declarative Salesforce configuration and reserve real engineering, QA, and a release cycle for what genuinely needs it. As one practitioner guide puts it, just because a customization is possible does not mean you should do it, because each one carries a cost to develop, test, and maintain.

How I would decide between directions: let the instrumentation data and the config-versus-build cost curve pick the next bet, not the loudest department.

Ship first

I'd start with the smallest useful step: making one number impossible to ignore, a cycle-time and error dashboard for the single highest-friction stage.

It breaks down by handoff, with a weekly read for the ops lead and the platform team. My hypothesis is that the valuation and underwriting-review step dominates the 14 to 30 day window, but I would confirm that from real timestamps before building anything else.

Why this first, in RICE terms: high confidence, moderate effort, and it de-risks every heavier bet by naming the real bottleneck.

What it deliberately leaves out: no automation, no AVM branching, no new intake tooling yet. Those wait until the data says where to point them. This is close to a dashboard I have already shipped, so I can move quickly on it.

Metrics

I would judge this work by outcomes the ops team feels, not by features shipped.

The North Star ties to the business constraint: funding more contracts without hiring one person per extra file.

Error and rework rate is a hard guardrail. Speeding up funding by trading away accuracy would be the wrong win as regulators tighten.
Genuine adoption is the second guardrail. A tool nobody trusts gets routed around, so I track real use, not logins.
The one metric I would not chase is feature or ticket count, which the JD explicitly rules out.

30 / 60 / 90

My first month would be mostly listening, because I am coming in humble about the specifics of this business.

Days 0 to 30 · listen

Shadow real files, map the workflows

Shadow underwriting, servicing, funding, and compliance through real files. Map the current intake queue and the existing Salesforce workflows. Sit with the Salesforce engineering manager to learn the config-versus-build cost curve and the release process. Sit with Analytics to see what is already instrumented. No big changes yet.

Days 30 to 60 · a quick win

Ship the dashboard, stand up intake

Ship the cycle-time and error dashboard for the highest-friction stage. Stand up a first pass at structured intake with the Product Operations Manager. Baseline the North Star and the guardrails.

Days 60 to 90 · validate and propose

Pick the first bet, defend a roadmap

Use the data to pick the first friction-removal bet, likely the valuation and review step, and validate it with a small slice. Propose a prioritized platform roadmap defended with data rather than request order.

Risks

The honest risks here sit in the work, and I would test the biggest one in week one.

RiskWhat I would validate first
The bottleneck assumption I am assuming the valuation and review stage dominates the 14 to 30 day window. Validate from real timestamps before building anything.
Config sprawl Per-state compliance across Illinois, Maine, and whatever the federal bill brings can turn into an unmaintainable patchwork of configuration exceptions. Validate the current config model with compliance and Salesforce engineering early.
Adoption A mandated internal tool with no buy-in gets routed around. Validate by co-designing with the ops staff who would use it, and by tracking real adoption, not logins.
Trust in the numbers Dashboards only help if the ops team trusts them. At Lentra the credibility of the data mattered as much as the data itself. Validate definitions and data quality with Analytics before publishing anything.
Intake politics Every department believes its request is most urgent. Validate a shared scoring model with the ops leads so prioritization is transparent, not imposed from above.

Questions

A handful of answers would reshape my plan, so I would ask them early.

  1. Is origination self-serve or assisted? Public reviews describe a roughly two-minute eligibility check followed by a human underwriting review with an appraisal. I would want to confirm where the human-in-the-loop steps sit and which one owns the most elapsed time.
  2. Is there a rule engine, and what does underwriting branch on? Splitero's stated inputs are credit score (minimum 500) and property appraisal, with no income or employment requirement. Does the workflow branch on an AVM confidence score the way modern HELOC stacks do, where AVM-based valuation can approve about 30% more loans and run about two days versus one to two weeks for a full appraisal, or is a full appraisal always required given the appreciation-sharing structure?
  3. After origination, is the path straight-through or not? Straight-through processing automates the lending lifecycle from application to disbursement, which frees skilled staff to focus on the more complex cases. What share of applications short-circuit manual review today, and what does the Salesforce routing logic look like for the rest?
  4. What regulatory framework applies to a non-bank HEI company? With Illinois, Maine, and pending federal action all moving in 2026 (overview), how does the compliance workflow adapt per-state disclosure and licensing requirements without becoming a config patchwork? That is exactly the config-versus-build trade-off this role owns.
