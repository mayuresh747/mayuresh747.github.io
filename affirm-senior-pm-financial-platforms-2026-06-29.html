The bet

Treat credit-bureau furnishing as a reconciliation problem, and cut its risk in the next two quarters.

Furnishing is Affirm's newest financial pipeline and its least governed, and that gap is now a live regulatory and audit risk. I would raise its coverage and drive exception volume and time-to-resolution down, the same measures the team already uses for settlement. It is the most bounded way to cut risk fast, and the reconcile-back pattern it produces extends to the broader settlement work and the future Affirm Bank reporting model.

What furnishing means

Furnishing means sending loan data to the credit bureaus on a regular cycle, so a consumer's credit file reflects their Affirm loans accurately and on time.

The problem

Furnishing became a permanent obligation across every Affirm loan, and its last step quietly goes missing.

Affirm began furnishing all pay-over-time loans to Experian on April 1, 2025 (Payments Dive) and to TransUnion on May 1, 2025 (TransUnion). That turned furnishing into a permanent operational obligation across every loan, including Pay-in-4 loans that were never reported before. The volume is large and growing: 45.3M transactions in the quarter ended March 31, 2026, up 45% year over year, with full-year GMV guidance near $49B (Affirm Q3 FY2026 8-K).

Who is hurt when a furnish is wrong

  • The consumer. A late or inaccurate furnish lands on a real person's credit file. FICO Score 10 BNPL launched in fall 2025, the first major scoring model to fold BNPL loans into credit scores (Benzinga). So a furnishing error is now a scoring error for one of 26.8M active consumers (8-K).
  • Compliance and Legal. In May 2026 a group of US senators wrote to the three bureaus demanding to know which BNPL providers furnish data and how, and pressing for consistent reporting standards (Payment Week). The regulatory ask is fundamentally an evidence ask: prove what you sent, when, and that it was correct.
  • Accounting and the auditor. Affirm reported its first GAAP-profitable quarter at $102.9M net income (Affirm Q3 FY2026 8-K), with an $18.4B platform portfolio (Affirm Q3 FY2026 results). Profitability and scale raise the bar on data accuracy and audit-trail completeness across every pipeline that feeds the financials.

Where the current approach can fail

Furnishing is a pipeline with five steps. Extract the point-in-time loan state, transform it to Metro 2 (the file format the bureaus require), transmit it, handle errors, and reconcile what the bureau actually accepted against what was sent. The last step is the one that quietly goes missing. If nobody matches the bureau's acknowledgment back to the source records, a rejected or dropped furnish sits invisible until a consumer disputes it or an auditor asks. That invisibility, not the transmission itself, is the risk.

Who it is for

This is an internal platform. A job to be done is what each user is hiring the furnishing pipeline to do, plus the consumer who is the indirect beneficiary.

User Job to be done
Furnishing operations analyst (Accounting / Compliance ops) When the monthly cycle runs, I want to confirm every furnishable loan landed correctly and see any rejection the same day, so I can fix it before it harms a consumer or surfaces in an audit.
Compliance and Legal partner When a regulator asks what we furnish and how accurately, I want a point-in-time, defensible answer with a full audit trail, so I respond with evidence, not estimates.
Consumer (indirect) When I pay an Affirm loan, I want my credit file to reflect it correctly and on time, so my score is accurate.

The decision-maker who funds this work is the leadership of the Financial Platforms org and the executives it reports to. The pitch to them is risk reduction with a measurable baseline, not a feature.

Furnishing wins because it pairs high reach and impact with the highest confidence and the most bounded effort. Approach

I would not pre-commit to a build. I would instrument first, find where errors actually originate, then invest where the data points.

1. Close the reconcile-back loop

Ingest the bureau's acknowledgment and rejection files, match them against what was sent, and turn every unmatched or rejected record into a tracked exception with an owner and a deadline. Bet: most of the risk is invisible because nobody reconciles accepted against sent today.

2. Validate before transmission with a data contract

A data contract is an agreed, enforced specification of what one system promises another: the fields, the format, and the meaning. I would catch Metro 2 format and loan-state errors at the boundary between Loans Lifecycle (the source of loan events) and the furnishing pipeline, before they ever reach the bureau. Bet: a meaningful share of rejects are preventable upstream.

3. Make furnishing observable

One view of coverage, exception volume, and time-to-resolution for operations, Compliance, and leadership, with a point-in-time audit trail. Bet: this is the prerequisite for the other two and it doubles as the regulatory evidence Compliance needs.

How I would decide: instrument the pipeline (direction 3 is cheap and first), classify a sample of rejects by origin, and let the evidence say whether the leverage is the missing loop (direction 1) or upstream validation (direction 2). I would not build all three on faith.

What I'd ship first

The smallest test of the biggest assumption: one bureau, one product, one monthly cycle.

The biggest assumption is that furnishing leaks in ways nobody currently sees, and that the leak is measurable and ownable. The smallest test of that assumption: a furnishing reconciliation ledger and exception view for one bureau and one product line, over one monthly cycle. I would start with Experian, the first bureau Affirm onboarded, and Pay-in-4, a product that was previously unreported. Reconcile what was sent against what the bureau acknowledged or rejected, classify every exception by origin, and assign an owner and a deadline to each.

Why first, in RICE terms: it is the smallest build that produces the baseline metrics (furnishing coverage and exception volume), proves or kills the invisible-leak hypothesis, and generates the exact evidence Compliance needs for a regulatory inquiry. High confidence, low effort, immediate impact.

What it deliberately leaves out

Automated upstream remediation, the second bureau, the bank-charter reporting model, and multi-product rollout. Those come after the baseline tells me where the real leverage is.

Metrics

North Star: furnishing reconciliation coverage. The share of furnishable loan records each cycle confirmed accepted by the bureau and matched to the source-of-truth loan state, point-in-time correct.

The guardrails matter. Coverage is easy to fake by quietly excluding the records that are hard to furnish, so I would track excluded-record volume as an anti-metric. Exceptions are easy to suppress by loosening validation, so I would watch the downstream dispute rate. And accuracy must not be bought with lateness, so furnish-by-date adherence stays on the board.

Quick win and baseline

  • Stand up the reconciliation ledger and exception view for one bureau and one product (the MVP).
  • Establish the first real coverage, exception-volume, and time-to-resolution numbers.
  • Agree the exception taxonomy, owners, and deadlines with the cross-functional partners.
  • Bring leadership the baseline and the gap.
Days 60 to 90

Ship, set targets, propose roadmap

  • Close the reconcile-back loop for the first slice, assign owners and deadlines, and cut the first measurable exception backlog.
  • Set the metric baseline and a target.
  • Propose the roadmap: second bureau, second product, the upstream data contract, and how the same pattern extends to settlement reconciliation and the future bank-charter reporting model.

Risks

These are open assumptions about the work, and the first thing I would test for each.

Open assumption in the work What I would validate first
The leak may already be small Week one, pull sent-versus-acknowledged for recent cycles and measure the unmatched rate. If it is already tight, point the same reconciliation discipline at the larger settlement surface.
Errors may originate upstream, not in furnishing A reject can come from a wrong loan state in Loans Lifecycle, not from the Metro 2 transformation. Before committing a build, classify a sample of rejects by origin.
Point-in-time data may be hard to reconstruct Reconciliation needs the loan state as it was at furnish time. Confirm that point-in-time loan history is available and trustworthy at the cycle's granularity.
Ownership may be ambiguous Furnishing spans Financial Platforms, Data Engineering, Compliance, and Legal. Confirm who owns furnishing accuracy today before designing the SLA.
A roadmap reorder is possible A bank-charter decision could change priorities. Design the reconcile-back pattern to be model-agnostic so it survives the transition rather than being rebuilt for it.

Questions

What I would ask the team in the first conversations.

  1. Which slice does this role own first: Loans Lifecycle, Money Movement, or a cross-cutting reconciliation and data-contract problem?
  2. What is today's furnishing exception rate and time-to-resolution, and who owns it now?
  3. Is there already a reconcile-back step that ingests bureau acknowledgment files, or is furnishing fire-and-forget once transmitted?
  4. How far along is the move to point-in-time loan history, and can we reconstruct furnish-time loan state today?
  5. What is the expected timeline on the Affirm Bank charter decision, and how much should the platform pre-build for a dual model now versus later?
  6. What evidence did Compliance and Legal need for the recent regulatory inquiry, and how long did it take to produce?
