The bet

Turn account health from after-the-fact punishment into clear, before-the-fact guidance, starting with the sellers who need it most.

Account health is still a guessing game for most sellers, and Amazon's own VP said so. My bet: I would raise the share of sellers who stay in the Healthy band and cut wrongful enforcement by stopping a violation before a listing goes live. The fastest move is a real-time compliance pre-check, measured against the team's own enforcement metrics, SSRI and HVLE False Positives.

The problem

Selling Partner Trust has to do two things that pull against each other: make sellers feel safe enough to grow, and enforce policy hard enough that bad actors cannot win. The friction built to stop bad actors also lands on legitimate sellers. That tension is the product.

Who hurts

The legitimate seller, especially the mid-sized one. 35% of Amazon sellers experienced an account suspension in 2024, and mid-sized businesses ($100K to $1M revenue) reported the highest rates (MyAmazonGuy, citing SmartScout Voice of the Amazon Seller, 2025; survey-based estimate, not an Amazon figure).

Why it happens

The Account Health Rating is a 0 to 1,000 score, and sellers drop into "At Risk" below 200 (SellerEngine AHR Guide, 2025). The rules tightened in 2025: smaller mistakes now cause larger drops, and proactive systems flag listings within hours. A common seller belief, that removing a listing fixes a violation, is wrong; sellers must file a formal Plan of Action. Sellers are scored on rules they do not fully understand, then asked to appeal in a process with no standardized timeline (Amazon Sellers Lawyer, 2025 to 2026).

The cost

A wrongful suspension freezes a seller's income while they wait. At the org level the team already tracks the cost as a board metric, HVLE False Positives, which means the over-enforcement problem is named and owned, not theoretical (SPT role description).

Three problems underneath, ranked

#ProblemEvidence
1 Account health reads like a guessing game. Sellers cannot tell why a score moved or what to do next. VP framing: "Account Health should feel like guidance, not a guessing game" (ZonHack, Amazon Accelerate 2025)

3 Regulatory compliance keeps cascading: GPSR, INFORM Act, EPR. Each is country-specific, and most sellers do not have a compliance team. Who it is for

This is a two-sided product. The daily user is the seller. The decision-maker who funds and judges the work is the SPT leadership chain (the VP of Selling Partner Trust, and SPS and Customer Trust under SVP Amit Agarwal, who reports to Worldwide Amazon Stores CEO Doug Herrington) (Amazon, Feb 2026).

The functional job is "stay compliant and sellable." The emotional job matters more for adoption: sellers want to feel safe, not surveilled. A compliance tool a seller does not trust enough to use fails no matter how good the model behind it is.

Prevention beats cure. Explainability tells a seller why they were hurt; a pre-check stops the harm. A real-time pre-check is the only option that can move both board metrics at once: it lifts SSRI indirectly (fewer legitimate sellers caught means fewer reinstatements) and attacks HVLE False Positives directly (fewer wrongful enforcements).

This direction also matches where Amazon is already heading. The Seller Assistant shown at Accelerate 2025 recognizes policy risk in real time and validates certificates within minutes (ZonHack, Accelerate 2025). It reached 230,000 monthly users with a recommendation acceptance rate above 90% in 2025, so sellers act on real-time guidance when it is good (Amazon 2025 Small Business Empowerment Report).

Approach

I would not commit to a build before testing the biggest assumption. Three directions, weighed against one decision test.

How I would decide: a two-week discovery against the team's data. I would pull the top reasons sellers enter At Risk and the top false-positive categories, then check which direction touches the largest share of those events. My hypothesis is that a narrow set of rule types drives most preventable enforcement, which makes a pre-check the highest-leverage place to start. If the data says most harm comes from post-listing behavioral signals (review velocity, shared infrastructure) rather than listing content, I would pivot the first slice toward explainability and an earlier human-in-the-loop check instead. The data decides, not the thesis.

What I would ship first

The smallest test of the biggest assumption. A real-time compliance pre-check for one seller cohort and one regulation. When a seller in the EU saves a non-food listing, the pre-check flags missing GPSR fields (EU Responsible Person, batch or serial number, local-language safety warnings) before the listing publishes, explains each gap in plain words, and offers the fix inline.

I would start with GPSR because the cost of getting it wrong is immediate and one-directional: GPSR has been enforced since December 13, 2024 with no grace period, and non-compliant listings are delisted (Amazon GPSR guide) (fluxy.one). That makes the before-and-after easy to measure: a blocked-at-publish gap is a delisting prevented.

The assumption this tests

Sellers will fix a flagged gap at the moment of listing if the guidance is clear and inline. The Seller Assistant acceptance rate above 90% suggests they will (Amazon 2025 Small Business Empowerment Report), but that is for recommendations, not a hard gate, so I would test it directly.

What the MVP deliberately leaves out

  • Behavioral false positives (review spikes, shared infrastructure).
  • The other regulations (EPR, INFORM).
  • Bulk tooling for high-value sellers, and the appeals workflow.

Those are the next slices, not the first. Holding scope to one cohort and one regulation is what makes the RICE effort low enough to ship and measure inside a quarter.

Metrics

North Star

The share of active sellers in the Healthy AHR band who got there without an enforcement event. It rises only when sellers are genuinely compliant, it moves before revenue, and the team can influence it.

Guardrails and anti-metrics

  • Do not chase raw flag volume. A pre-check that flags everything looks busy and trains sellers to ignore it. I would track flag precision (share of flags a seller agrees with) as a guardrail.
  • Do not let enforcement accuracy fall while chasing fewer false positives. The two move against each other, so I would watch true-positive enforcement rate alongside HVLE False Positives.
  • Do not optimize appeals speed alone. Fast appeals on a wrongful suspension still means the harm happened first.

30 / 60 / 90

Days 0 to 30 Listen and find the real constraint

Read the existing roadmap and the last few SPT six-pagers. Sit with Account Health Specialists and watch real seller cases. Interview sellers in both cohorts using past behavior, not hypotheticals: "walk me through the last time a listing got flagged." Pull the data on what drives sellers into At Risk and what drives false positives, and find which rule types are most preventable at the point of listing.

Days 30 to 60 A quick win and a sharpened problem

Confirm or correct the pre-check hypothesis with month-one data. Ship a small, visible win, most likely AHR explainability copy that tells a seller why a specific score moved and the exact next step, since that needs little engineering and builds trust while the pre-check is scoped. Write the PRD for the GPSR pre-check MVP, align engineering on technical design, and get leadership buy-in in a six-pager that ties the work to SSRI and HVLE False Positives.

Days 60 to 90 Ship and validate the first bet

Launch the GPSR pre-check to a limited EU seller group. Set the baseline for the North Star and the early metrics. Measure fix rate and precision against the plan's targets, with explicit kill criteria so the pilot can end if sellers ignore the flags. Use the result to propose the next slices (the next regulation, then the behavioral false-positive problem) on the same RICE scale.

Risks

Open questions about the proposed work, and how I would test each in week one.

Risk in the workHow I would validate it first
The biggest source of preventable harm may be behavioral signals, not listing content. A content pre-check would then move the metric less than hoped. Pull the top false-positive categories in week one and check the content-versus-behavior split before committing the build.
A pre-check that gates publishing could add friction and slow legitimate sellers, the opposite of the goal. Run the first version as a warning, not a hard block, and measure fix rate and drop-off before deciding whether to gate.
Country-specific regulation makes a worldwide build hard. GPSR logic does not transfer cleanly to EPR or INFORM. Scope the MVP to one regulation and one region, and design the rule layer so adding a country is configuration, not a rebuild. Test that with engineering in the first design review.
Tightening one board metric can worsen the other. Cutting false positives can let real violations through. Instrument both true-positive enforcement and false positives from day one, and treat enforcement accuracy as a guardrail the pre-check cannot breach.
Scope ambiguity: this role may own the AHR dashboard, the compliance tooling, the Seller Assistant, or a mix. Confirm the exact product boundary with the hiring manager in week one and re-rank the RICE table against the real scope.

Questions

What I would ask the team.

  1. Which surface does this role actually own first: the AHR dashboard, the regulatory compliance tooling, the Seller Assistant, or a defined slice of all three?
  2. Of current high-value-listing enforcements flagged as false positives, what share trace to listing content versus behavioral signals? That ratio changes where I would start.
  3. Where is the line between this role's product work and the enforcement systems that decide what gets flagged? How much can a PM here influence the model's risk appetite?
  4. Is the first build scoped worldwide from day one, or do we earn the right to expand region by region after one regulation proves out?
  5. What does leadership consider a credible quarterly move on SSRI and HVLE False Positives, so I can set MVP targets against a real bar?
  6. With the VP role in transition, how settled is the current roadmap, and how much latitude does this PM have to reshape near-term priorities?
