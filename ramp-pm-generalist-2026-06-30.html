Team 01 · Spend Management

Owns Ramp’s core spend product: card issuance, reimbursements, and travel & entertainment controls.

The bet

Ramp checks policy in real time for money that flows through its own cards, but spend that starts off-platform stays invisible until a reimbursement arrives days later.

Out-of-pocket meals, off-platform hotels, personal-card SaaS trials, and AI token bills all bypass the enforcement engine. My bet: grow the share of company spend that gets checked against policy before or at the moment it happens, by pulling off-platform and out-of-pocket spend into the same real-time enforcement loop that already covers cards. That closes the gap where competitors are attacking, and it compounds the metric Ramp already leads on.

The problem

Real-time control stops at the edge of Ramp’s rails.

Ramp built the best policy enforcement in this market for card spend. Its Policy Agent catches 7x more out-of-policy spend than rules-based tools, and it lets reviewers touch only 10 to 15% of transactions Ramp Policy Agent GA, Jan 2026, enforcing policy at 99% accuracy Ramp Agents, Jul 2025. Across 50,000+ businesses, out-of-policy spend events fell 62% over two years The Ramp Effect, Mar 2026. That is real and it is Ramp’s moat.

Here is the gap. That protection only reaches spend that runs through a Ramp card. Two large channels bypass it.

  • Off-platform travel. 80% of business travelers sometimes book outside their company’s tool Navan, State of Corporate Travel and Expense 2026. Ramp Travel covers flights and hotels via a single partner, with no rail, no GDS inventory, and no 24/7 support. Travelers who want a full booking experience go elsewhere, and that spend disappears from Ramp’s view until it comes back as a reimbursement.
  • Out-of-pocket spend. The “dark matter” of travel and entertainment. An employee pays personally, then must remember to photograph a receipt, categorize it, justify it, and wait for approval, then wait one to two more days for payment. Every step is a place to drop off. 29% of organizations still process expense reports manually, up from 23% the prior year, and travel and expense “operates on a delay” because it is reconciled through approval chains that take weeks Navan, Apr 2026.

The root problem. Ramp runs a two-tier control model. Card spend is governed the instant it happens. The long tail is governed after the fact, if at all. Finance leads who moved from chasing 100% of expenses to reviewing 10 to 15% still cannot see the off-platform 80% until it lands in a queue. The headline that Policy Agent catches 7x more out-of-policy spend is, read the other way, an admission that only card-originated spend gets that protection.

This is where the competition is aiming. Navan’s Expense Chat (March 2026) lets users drop receipt images into a chat that extracts, codes, and files reimbursements automatically, and Brex auto-fetches receipts. Both target the exact friction Ramp has not yet removed.

Approach

I would not commit to a solution before testing the assumption underneath it. Three directions, each a bet I would validate.

  1. Capture-first: kill submission friction. A conversational or camera-first capture flow where an employee drops a receipt and the agent extracts, codes, and files it, checked against policy at capture time rather than at review. This is the direct answer to Navan’s Expense Chat. Bet: the drop-off is friction, not intent. Decide by measuring completion and time-to-submit against today’s flow.
  2. Prevention-first: replace out-of-pocket with scoped cards. Push single-use virtual cards, and the Agent Card primitive, into moments that today default to personal spend: a per-trip card at booking, a scoped card for a SaaS trial, an auto-expiring card for an agent’s task Agent Cards, Mar 2026. Bet: much out-of-pocket spend is out-of-pocket only because issuing a card was slower than reaching for a personal one. Decide by measuring how much reimbursement volume shifts to card spend when a scoped card is one tap away.
  3. Visibility-first: pull the blind channels in. Connect the data Ramp does not see: off-platform bookings via itinerary and email parsing, and AI token usage pulled straight from providers, which Ramp already does with AI Token Spend Management. Bet: even without controlling the transaction, surfacing it early lets Policy Agent flag it before month-end.

How I would decide. Run all three as small probes in the first 60 days, then double down on whichever moves the North Star per unit of effort. My prior is that prevention (bet 2) has the highest ceiling because it removes the problem instead of processing it, but capture (bet 1) is the fastest to prove and blunts the competitive wedge, so I would ship a capture probe first while building the prevention path.

What I would ship first

The smallest test of the biggest assumption: that off-platform and out-of-pocket spend can be brought into real-time policy checking with a flow employees actually complete.

MVP: a capture-to-policy flow on one high-volume channel. Pick the out-of-pocket meal-and-entertainment receipt, the highest-frequency out-of-pocket category. An employee drops a receipt image (or forwards a confirmation email), the agent extracts and codes it, and Policy Agent evaluates it against policy at that moment, returning an in-policy or flagged result before the employee closes the app, not weeks later at review.

Why this first, in RICE terms: highest Reach (nearly every employee files these), high Impact (extends real-time enforcement to a channel that has none), and the highest Confidence-over-Effort because Ramp already owns OCR, coding, and the Policy Agent evaluation. The reused engine is why this is weeks of stitching, not a new system.

What it deliberately leaves out: travel inventory depth, international card issuance, and full agentic-spend governance. Those are real and they come next. The MVP is not the whole loop; it is the proof that the loop closes on one channel with a flow people finish.

How I would measure success

North Star: share of company spend evaluated against policy before or at the moment of purchase. Today this is high for card spend and near zero for off-platform and out-of-pocket. Moving it is the whole thesis, and it ladders directly to Ramp’s headline outcome of customer savings and to the “control spend” charter.

Guardrails and anti-metrics, so I do not win the North Star by cheating quality.

  • Policy Agent accuracy stays above 99%. Pulling in messy off-platform data must not degrade the model that reviewers trust.
  • False-flag rate does not rise. If we flag more spend by flagging good spend, reviewers stop trusting the queue and go back to reviewing everything.
  • Anti-metric: employee reimbursement time does not grow. If capturing more spend earlier slows down getting people paid, employees route around us and the data disappears again. Ramp pays reimbursements in one to two business days today; that must not regress.

Team 02 · Procurement

Owns Ramp’s procurement platform: vendor management and spend controls.

The bet

The next unit of value in procurement is the renewal, and it is where Ramp is most exposed. I would take Renewal and Contract Intelligence from alpha to a consumption-aware engine.

Ramp’s procurement platform saves customers 16% a year and runs 3x faster since the April 2026 AI agent launch CPA Practice Advisor. My bet: build an engine that uses Ramp’s own card and bill-pay data to benchmark the metered, AI-driven spend that seat-based benchmarks cannot price. That is a signal no competitor has.

The problem

The renewal is the most cost-consequential moment in vendor management, and it is getting slower and riskier.

  • The average company runs 211 SaaS renewals a year, roughly four a week, across 305 applications, and still wastes $19.8M a year on unused licenses Zylo 2026 SaaS Management Index.
  • Renewals now take 82 days versus 40 days for new purchases, and the gap is widening as legal teams add AI data-privacy clauses Vertice.
  • 79% of IT leaders hit a price increase at their last renewal, and 61% of organizations cut projects because of unplanned SaaS cost increases Zylo 2026 SaaS Pricing Trends.

The root problem. Software is moving from per-seat to consumption-based billing, so 78% of IT leaders saw unexpected charges tied to consumption or AI features in the past year Zylo. Ramp’s Price Intelligence is built on transaction data, which prices a seat well but cannot price a metered contract where there is no seat to compare.

Ramp’s own data shows monthly AI spend per firm is up 4x year over year, and 80% of companies miss their AI spend forecast by 25% or more Ramp. Today teams cope with spreadsheets, calendar reminders, and a scramble 30 days before expiry. Those workarounds fail because the buyer walks into the renewal with no leverage: no current usage data, no benchmark for consumption pricing, no early warning. The cost is the $19.8M of waste and the 61% of cut projects above.

Who it is for

Three roles feel this, and the buyer is not the daily user.

  • Finance and procurement lead (economic buyer). Owns the vendor budget, signs the renewal, and answers to the CFO for overspend. The one who approves paying for Ramp Procurement.
  • Budget owner or business-unit leader (the daily user and the source of shadow IT). Most software today is bought by lines of business rather than central IT, so these leaders buy tools without procurement in the loop.
  • The requesting employee (the front door). Median requisition-to-PO is 55 hours, up from 51 in 2024 Vertice, via brief. If intake is slow they expense it, which is how shadow IT starts.

Approach

Three bets, and I let evidence pick where the consumption benchmark comes from.

The core question is where the consumption benchmark comes from, because that is the piece no seat-based system has. I would run three directions and let evidence pick.

  • Bet A, data-native benchmark. Ramp uniquely sees actual metered spend, because those invoices flow through Ramp AP. Build the consumption benchmark from that history. This is the defensible moat, but only if the signal is dense enough. I would decide with a coverage test: for the top 50 consumption-priced vendors, can we produce a benchmark that lands within a stated variance against contracts we already know the outcome of?
  • Bet B, blended usage. Where Ramp’s own signal is thin, pull usage from Seat Intelligence and connected tools to fill gaps. This trades some purity for coverage. I would ship it where Bet A’s coverage test fails but a connected source exists.
  • Bet C, honest fallback. Where we have neither, show usage and flagged terms but no benchmark, and say so plainly. Ramp’s own docs already caveat that Price Intelligence “may be based on incomplete or outdated data” brief. Tropic attacks data-derived benchmarks directly. The trust move is to never fabricate a number the data cannot support.

The deciding rule across all three: a benchmark ships only if it clears a variance bar against known outcomes. Below the bar, we surface usage and terms, not a guess.

What I would ship first

The smallest test of the biggest assumption: is Ramp’s data dense enough to price consumption contracts at all?

I would not ship a full renewal cockpit first. I would ship a consumption benchmark accuracy probe on a narrow slice.

  • Scope: the top consumption-priced vendors where Ramp AP already sees metered invoices (AI tools, cloud, data). For each, generate a benchmark plus a current-usage read, delivered as the negotiation brief inside the existing alpha renewal feature, 90 days before expiry.
  • Why first (RICE reasoning): highest impact moment, reuses the shipped agent infrastructure and existing card data, so effort is contained. Confidence is the open question, which is exactly why the MVP is built to measure it.
  • What it deliberately leaves out: zero-touch sourcing, non-metered categories, integrations beyond what is already connected, and any autonomous commitment. The agent drafts the brief; a human still signs. This MVP answers one question: can Ramp price consumption spend from its own data well enough to change a negotiation.

How I would measure success

North Star: renewal savings captured through Ramp.

The North Star means dollars saved on renewals where a Ramp benchmark or usage read informed the outcome. It passes the three tests: if it rises the customer genuinely won, it moves before revenue, and the team can influence it directly. It extends the existing 16% headline into the consumption era rather than inventing a vanity number.

  • Anti-metric: agent action count. Do not chase the number of steps an agent runs. Zip reports 58M requests a year with 30% autonomous brief; action volume looks impressive and says nothing about whether the customer paid less. Optimize savings, not activity.

Team 03 · Treasury

Owns cash management products that optimize liquidity and returns on corporate cash.

The bet

The fastest way to grow treasury assets and yield per customer is to give the AI a complete view of every cash obligation, on and off Ramp, so it can sweep more aggressively without ever risking a payroll miss.

Ramp holds $5B in treasury assets and already runs an autonomous sweep that moves idle cash into yield Ramp business banking. The next leg of growth is stuck on one thing: the AI can only see the cash obligations that flow through Ramp, so users keep large idle buffers “just in case.”

The problem

Real-time cash visibility stops at the edge of Ramp’s rails, so users park idle buffers rather than risk a shortfall.

Who hurts. The controller or CFO at a 50 to 500 person company with no dedicated treasury team. They track cash across two to four accounts (operating, payroll, tax reserve, investment) in a spreadsheet and move money between them by hand, roughly monthly.

What it costs them. They know cash earns about 3.5% in a government money-market fund, and their checking earns almost nothing. The national average business checking rate is 0.07% FDIC, March 2026, via the brief. On a $2M operating balance, the gap is roughly $68,000 in forgone yield a year.

The root cause. Users do not have a continuous, real-time view of how much cash they can safely deploy without risking a shortfall. Ramp’s forecast looks 13 weeks ahead at bills, card statements, and reimbursements, but it does not automatically know payroll, debt service, quarterly taxes, or externally-initiated direct debits. The support docs say the forecast “does not automatically know every off-Ramp obligation,” so users must add each one by hand as a custom line item.

The workaround, and why it fails. Users do one of two things. They keep a big idle buffer in checking so they never get caught short, which defeats the whole point and leaves yield on the table. Or they turn on automations and accept the risk that a sweep pulls cash needed for payroll in three days, into an account that takes three days to settle. Payroll is usually the single largest outflow, $250K to $2M per run. A timing mismatch between a sweep and a payroll run is a real source of user anxiety, and a real failure mode.

Why now. The Fed has held rates in the 3.50 to 3.75% range for a fourth straight meeting, with the effective federal funds rate in the mid-3% range in mid-2026 Federal Reserve H.15. Elevated rates have lasted far longer than the post-2008 norm, so the incentive to optimize cash is large and durable. Meanwhile Capital One closed its $5.15B acquisition of Brex in April 2026, the largest bank-fintech deal in history, and Brex carried $13B in deposits at close TechCrunch. That integration creates churn, and Ramp is the obvious landing spot for a company that already uses corporate cards and bill pay.

Who it is for

Primary user: the hands-on finance owner. The controller or CFO who personally moves the money. They are not a treasury specialist. They want to earn market yield without becoming one.

The job to be done

When I have cash sitting in the bank, help me put as much of it as possible to work at a good rate, without ever leaving me short for payroll, rent, or taxes.

The tension

The two halves are in tension. More yield means more cash deployed; more safety means more cash idle. The product’s job is to hold both at once.

The economic buyer is usually the same person at this company size, or their CFO one level up. That simplifies the sale but raises the bar on trust: the person who authorizes autonomous money movement is the same person who gets fired if payroll bounces.

Approach

The core hypothesis: if Ramp handles disruption inside the product, travelers stay on platform, in-policy booking holds, and the incremental expense files itself.

Green steps use capabilities Ramp already has (SMS and Slack channels, Priceline booking, Policy Agent expense logic). Yellow steps are the real build: real-time airline status, waiver-aware rebooking logic, and a "trip as a unit" abstraction the Policy Agent lacks today.

Three directions I would explore, and the decision rule for each:

  1. Where does trip-level intelligence live? Extend the Policy Agent, build a separate Trip Agent, or put it in a shared data layer. Decision rule: whichever gives the cleanest "one trip, one record" abstraction that both booking and expense can read, without forking the Policy Agent's 99% accuracy. I would prototype the data layer first because both bets depend on it.

What I would ship first

The smallest test of the biggest assumption: that travelers will let Ramp rebook them if Ramp reaches them at the right moment with an in-policy option.

So the MVP is a flight-disruption watch-and-rebook agent for hotel and air, confirmation-gated, on the existing Priceline inventory and the existing SMS/Slack channels.

What it does: watch booked itineraries for cancellations and major delays, detect the disruption, surface one or two in-policy rebooking options, and let the traveler confirm with one tap. On confirmation, it rebooks and auto-generates the amended expense with an itinerary-change memo.

Why this first, in RICE terms: highest reach (disruption touches most trips), high impact (it directly protects in-policy rate and recovers the +27% overspend), and the confidence is high because it reuses Ramp's channels, policy rules, and expense logic. Effort is contained by scoping to confirmation-gated rebooking on existing inventory.

What it deliberately leaves out: full autonomy (no auto-rebook without a tap yet), car and rail (no inventory today), NDC self-service changes (only 13% of booking tools support these, so it is a separate hard problem), and 24/7 human support. Those are follow-ons, not the first test.

How I would measure success

The North Star is disruption recovery rate: for trips that hit a cancellation or major delay, the share recovered by in-product rebooking rather than the traveler going off to a consumer site. It is the tightest signal that the middle of the trip is now covered, and it ladders directly to attach, in-policy rate, and expense automation.

Guardrails and anti-metrics keep the agent honest:

  • Rebooking accuracy (guardrail): share of agent rebookings that are correct and in-policy. A disruption agent that rebooks wrong is worse than no agent. I would hold this to Policy Agent-class accuracy before raising autonomy.
  • Traveler opt-out / trust (anti-metric): if travelers turn the agent off or ignore its messages, recovery rate is a vanity number. I would watch opt-out and message-ignore rates as the honesty check.
  • Expense automation rate as a supporting input, benchmarked toward Navan's 73% auto-reconcile figure and then beyond, since the amended expense should file itself.
