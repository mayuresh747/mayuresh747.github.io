The bet

My bet: UW already shipped the AI capability, so the scarce thing now is proof the agents get adopted, measured, and kept safe across a decentralized campus.

Purple, UW's enterprise generative AI tool, went live to faculty and staff on June 16, 2026, and already advertises custom AI agents as a headline feature (Purple is here, UW-IT). Shipping a capability is not the same as shipping agents people trust and use.

So here is what I would do: pick one high-volume, document-heavy administrative workflow, ship a grounded, cited agent that resolves the routine half of the load and hands the rest to a human, with evaluation, guardrails, and Washington records-retention built in from the first commit.

The University of Hawai'i system shows the shape is real. 51% of students engaged its chatbots and sent 100,000+ messages, with roughly 1,900 questions answered without a person and about 165 staff hours saved (UH News, Apr 2026). Prove that pattern on one workflow, then turn it into a template other departments reuse. That is how UW's own goal to launch a vetted, continuously updated AI portfolio (UW-IT AI Strategy) becomes measured hours returned instead of a demo.

The problem

The gap I'd attack is the distance between a capability that exists and agents that staff actually rely on.

  • Who hurts. Frontline staff across a decentralized university (three campuses, UW Medicine, global research) answer the same routine requests all day and do manual lookups across Workday, Canvas, and policy PDFs. The requester, whether a faculty member, staff member, or student, waits in a ticket queue for an answer that already exists somewhere.
  • What it costs, concretely. UW replaced 850+ legacy finance and HR processes with Workday in a $340 million program. After go-live the supplier-invoice backlog peaked near $90 million and was still $43 million in early 2024, with hundreds of grant awards stuck in processing (The Register, 2024). UW-IT's own update later reported the balance down to $25 million with outside consultants brought in to remediate (Workday Finance Stabilization Update). That surge of "where is my invoice, how do I submit this" work is exactly the repetitive load an agent can absorb.
  • Root cause. The knowledge to answer these requests is scattered across enterprise systems and long documents, and the only path today is a human reading and re-explaining it.
  • Workarounds, and why they fail. Staff ask a colleague, dig through a wiki, or open a ticket. These have zero switching cost and are fully trusted, which is precisely why a new tool has to be clearly better, not marginally better, to displace them.
  • The trap that makes this hard. Every agent that logs a prompt or an output is a potential public record under Washington's Public Records Act (RCW 42.56), and both prompts and outputs can qualify (Schwabe analysis). UW's records office says GenAI platforms are not recordkeeping repositories and has partnered with UW-IT to enable auto-deletion on approved tools (UW Records Management AI guidance). Purple itself is barred from HIPAA, UW Medicine, CUI, and confidential research data (Purple is here), and the student-records system (SDB/Keynes) is FERPA-governed with role-based access (UW Registrar). An agent that ignores any of this is a liability, not a win.

Who it is for

I'd build first for the staffer drowning in the same request all day, and the requester stuck behind them.

This is internal work for a public institution, so the daily user is not the buyer. The staffer uses it, leadership funds it, and Records and Privacy can veto it. All three have to be satisfied.

Approach

I see three orchestration directions, and I'd let a short evaluation decide, not a preference.

  • Direction 1 · extend PurpleExtend Purple's native agent feature. Lowest new surface area, stays inside UW's Azure tenant, inherits Purple's data boundary. Open question: what the feature exposes for authoring, templates, logging, and evaluation is not public, so I'd map it in week one.
  • Direction 2 · commercial gatewayConfigure a commercial gateway (nebulaONE). The JD names it as highly desirable. It is a real Azure-native GenAI gateway that routes across model providers and adds governance and cost controls (Cloudforce), and higher-ed peers move fast on it: UCLA Anderson launched its first chatbot within two months of adoption (eCampus News), and Oxford's pilot pitches no-code custom chatbots for staff (Oxford AI Centre). Trade-off: license cost and a dependency on an outside vendor.
  • Direction 3 · build in-houseBuild lightweight in-house orchestration (a role-based framework such as CrewAI). Most control, cheapest license, most maintenance load.

How I'd decide: score all three on the same axes I already use in my UW research work: capability, ingestion cost, query cost, governance fit, and time-to-first-agent. UW's deepened Microsoft partnership makes Azure the default substrate to build against (UW and Microsoft, Feb 2026), which weights the first two options above a fully custom build.

Whichever wins, the agent itself follows one shape: retrieve, ground, cite, check confidence, and escalate to a human when unsure. The escalation branch is where trust is won.

Ship first

The first thing I'd ship is deliberately small: one agent, one workflow, one bounded corpus.

The biggest assumption to test: a grounded, cited agent can resolve enough routine volume in a real UW workflow to return meaningful staff hours, while staying inside FERPA and records rules, and staff will trust it enough to come back.

The MVP: one department's high-volume question set, answered over a bounded, low-sensitivity document corpus, deployed on Purple/Azure. Answers carry citations. A confidence threshold routes anything uncertain to a human instead of guessing. Prompt-and-output logging is wired to the approved auto-deletion path from day one, before the agent handles anything real. Run it as a 4-to-6-week pilot with an explicit success bar and kill criteria.

What it deliberately leaves out (per the RICE reasoning):

  • No write-actions into systems of record. Read-only first. No Workday or SDB writes.
  • No FERPA or restricted data in v1. Public and low-sensitivity content only, so the pilot clears Privacy review quickly.
  • No multi-agent orchestration and no self-service authoring. Those are phase 2, after one agent proves the pattern.

Metrics

I'd judge this on routine requests resolved with a trustworthy answer and no human touch, not on message volume.

The North Star rises only when staff time is genuinely returned and the answer was trustworthy. Raw message volume is an anti-metric: it can climb while value falls.

Groundedness and hallucination rate against a labeled review set. This is the exact evaluation I already run on policy text in my UW research.
Escalate-when-unsure rate. The agent should hand off when it is not sure, not paper over gaps to keep the deflection number up.
Zero restricted-data exposures, and 100% of prompts and outputs on the approved retention path. These are pass/fail, not targets to trend.
Cost per resolved request. Purple is free to faculty and staff, but Azure token and compute cost is real, and a gateway license would add to it.
Anti-metric: raw message volume. It can climb while value falls, so I would not chase it.

30 / 60 / 90

My first quarter would move from listening to a measured pilot.

Days 0 to 30 · listen and map

Learn the constraints before touching anything

  • Meet the AI Platforms team and the Technology Manager. Read UW's AI Guidelines, the records-management GenAI guidance, and Purple's data-boundary rules.
  • Map what Purple's custom-agent feature actually does today: authoring, templates, logging, evaluation hooks.
  • Shortlist 2 to 3 candidate workflows by sitting with the busiest service desks (Workday/finance stabilization, HR, IT/ServiceNow) and pulling real request volumes.
  • Get the FERPA, Public Records, and retention constraints from Records Management and UW Privacy in writing.
  • Stand up a throwaway dev agent in the Azure tenant over a small public corpus to learn the plumbing.
Days 30 to 60 · quick win and sharpen

Build the MVP agent and the evaluation harness

  • Pick the one workflow. Replace my RICE estimates with the real volume and hours data.
  • Build the MVP agent over a bounded, low-sensitivity corpus: citations, confidence-based escalation, retention logging.
  • Stand up the evaluation harness (groundedness and hallucination review set), reusing the method from my research work.
  • Align the department sponsor and Records/Privacy on pilot scope, success bar, and kill criteria.
  • Run the nebulaONE-versus-build spike in parallel and write it up as a one-page decision memo.
Days 60 to 90 · ship, baseline, propose

Run the pilot and turn it into a template

  • Run the 4-to-6-week pilot against the defined success and kill bar.
  • Baseline the North Star, staff hours returned, groundedness, and cost per request.
  • If it clears the bar, extract the reusable pieces (retrieval config, guardrail policy, retention wiring, evaluation harness) into a starter template so a second, deliberately different department goes faster.
  • Propose the phased roadmap: more workflows, then self-service authoring behind Purple, with the buy-vs-build recommendation attached.

Risks

Every risk here is about the work itself, and I'd test the biggest ones in week one.

RiskWhat I would validate first
Data boundary The chosen workflow may touch FERPA or restricted data sooner than expected. Validate corpus sensitivity with Records and Privacy in week one, and start read-only and low-sensitivity.
Records and Public Records exposure Every logged prompt and output may be a public record. Validate the approved auto-deletion path before the agent logs anything real.
Adoption Staff may not trust or return to the agent, and asking a colleague has zero switching cost. Validate with a small pilot group and a repeat-use metric, not a launch announcement.
Retrieval quality Enterprise and policy text is messy and groundedness may be too low to trust. Validate against a labeled review set before the pilot, not after.
Buy vs build Purple-native, nebulaONE, and a custom framework could each be right. Validate on capability, ingestion cost, query cost, and governance fit before committing.
Purple dependency What the native agent feature exposes is unclear. Validate what the platform actually gives before deciding to extend it or build alongside it.
Decentralization A template that fits one department may not fit the next. Validate by choosing the second department deliberately unlike the first.

Questions

A few answers would change my plan, so I'd ask them early.

  1. Is nebulaONE actually licensed or piloted at UW, or named in the JD as a category example? The answer decides whether I configure a gateway or build orchestration.
  2. What does Purple's custom-agent feature expose today (authoring, templates, logging, evaluation hooks), and is this role meant to extend it or build alongside it?
  3. Which workflow does leadership most want moved first, and is there a department sponsor already asking? Workday stabilization, an IT/ServiceNow desk, or a specific college?
  4. What is the approved records-retention and auto-deletion path for agent prompts and outputs, and who signs off on FERPA and restricted-data scope?
  5. What is the year-one success bar for the AI-portfolio milestone: adoption, hours saved, cost, safety, or all of them, and against what baseline?
